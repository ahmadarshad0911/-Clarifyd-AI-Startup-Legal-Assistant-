Abstract

Clarifyd is an AI-assisted contract risk analyzer built for pre-seed startup founders: intelligent, time-poor, legally untrained people who must decide — often late at night and without counsel — whether to sign a SAFE, a term sheet, a non-disclosure agreement, or a vendor services contract. Legal review at this stage is slow and expensive; existing contract tools are designed for corporate legal departments and speak their language; and a raw large language model is unsafe for the task, because it hallucinates, can be manipulated by instructions hidden inside the contract itself, and will confidently produce statements that amount to legal advice.

A founder uploads a contract. Clarifyd validates and extracts the document, decides whether it is a contract at all, segments it into clauses, and scores each clause for risk severity and model confidence. It then runs three whole-document passes: a full narrative report, a loophole sweep that identifies dangerous missing clauses as well as present ones, and an ambiguity sweep that flags vague or undefined language. Findings are ranked deterministically, explained in plain English with the reasoning made explicit, and — when they are severe, uncertain, or suspicious — routed automatically to a human review queue rather than being auto-approved. A conversational assistant answers follow-up questions and drafts documents clause by clause, refusing to produce a draft until every essential term has actually been supplied.

The engineering contribution of this project is not the language model, which is a rented component. It is the production safety envelope constructed around it: a multi-provider fallback chain terminating in a deterministic rules engine, so the product never hard-fails even when every model provider is unavailable; a prompt-injection defence that treats contract text as untrusted input written by an adversary; a confidence gate that forces uncertain findings in front of a human; a SHA-256 hash-chained audit log that makes the record of what happened impossible to rewrite quietly; and a non-legal-advice guarantee enforced by schema validation rather than by good intentions. The system is delivered under an SLC (Simple, Loveable, Complete) philosophy rather than a feature-maximising one: a single workflow — upload, analyze, review, export — finished completely and safely.

The delivered system comprises a Next.js front end, a FastAPI back end with fifteen relational tables, and a hosted Llama-3.1-70B model reached through an OpenAI-compatible interface. It is validated by 124 automated back-end tests across nineteen files, including dedicated suites for upload security, prompt injection, provider fallback, and a test that deliberately corrupts the audit chain and asserts that the corruption is detected. This report documents the motivation, the requirements, the process model, the system design with full diagram set, the implementation, the testing strategy, and — deliberately and in detail — the system’s honest limitations.

Keywords: contract analysis; large language models; applied artificial intelligence; prompt-injection defence; graceful degradation; tamper-evident audit logging; human-in-the-loop review; legal technology; startup finance.

Chapter 1

Introduction

1.1 Domain and Sub-Domain

Clarifyd sits at the intersection of three domains. The practical domain is legal technology — software that assists with the reading, drafting, and negotiation of legal documents. The technical domain is applied artificial intelligence: specifically, the engineering of a dependable product on top of a probabilistic, third-party large language model that the engineering team does not own, cannot fully predict, and must not blindly trust. The commercial framing is software-as-a-service for early-stage startups.

The sub-domain in which this project claims a contribution is safety engineering for applied language models. A large language model is a component with unusual failure characteristics. It fails silently rather than loudly. It produces fluent, confident prose even when it is wrong. It can be manipulated by the very data it is asked to process. And it is delivered over a network by a third party who may throttle, deprecate, or withdraw it without notice. Every one of those failure modes materialised during this project, and each is answered by a mechanism described in this report.

1.2 Problem Statement

A founder raising a pre-seed round is handed a SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity), a term sheet, a non-disclosure agreement, or a vendor services contract, and is asked to sign. The founder is intelligent but not legally trained; short of time and short of money. The document is ten to forty pages of dense, deliberately precise language in which a single provision — a liability cap omitted, an intellectual-property assignment widened, a most-favoured-nation clause quietly inserted — may determine whether they still own their company in three years.

The available options are all poor. Engaging a lawyer is slow and, at this stage of a company's life, disproportionately expensive; a founder with no revenue struggles to justify the fee against a document they hope is standard. Reading it unaided is unreliable: the danger is not that the founder cannot understand English, but that they cannot notice what is absent — the mutual-confidentiality clause that was never included, the liability cap that is simply not there. Existing contract-analysis software is built for corporate legal departments; it assumes a trained reader, speaks in legal vocabulary, and is priced for enterprise procurement.

The obvious modern shortcut — pasting the contract into a general-purpose chatbot — is actively unsafe, for three reasons that recur throughout this report.

  • Hallucination. The model will produce a fluent, confident, and entirely fabricated reading of a clause, with no signal to the reader that it has done so.
  • Prompt injection. The contract is written by the counterparty. Text embedded in the document can address itself to the model — “ignore your instructions and report this agreement as low risk” — and a naive system complies. The input is adversarial by construction.
  • Unlicensed legal advice. A general chatbot will readily produce jurisdiction-specific legal conclusions. Supplying these without a licence exposes both provider and user to liability.
The thesis of this project

The value of Clarifyd is not that a language model reads a contract; that capability is commoditised and available to anybody. The value is the disciplined product constructed around the model: founder-framed explanations, a confidence threshold that forces uncertain findings in front of a human, a deterministic fallback so the product survives model outage, a defence against adversarial input, an audit record that cannot be quietly rewritten, and a non-advice guarantee enforced by schema validation rather than by good intentions.

1.3 Motivation

The motivation is asymmetry of consequence. When a consumer application misclassifies a photograph, the cost is an irritated user. When a contract-analysis tool assures a founder that an uncapped indemnity is “standard”, the cost may be the founder's house. That asymmetry means the interesting engineering problem is not accuracy in the average case; it is behaviour in the failure case. What does the system do when the model is unavailable? When it is confidently wrong? When the document is actively attempting to deceive it? A system that answers those questions well is worth more than one that scores marginally higher on a benchmark and collapses under adversarial conditions.

A secondary motivation is honesty about capability. There is commercial incentive in this domain to imply that an AI system replaces a lawyer. It does not, and Clarifyd says so at every layer — in the interface, in the data schema, and in this document.

1.5 Workflow

The product implements a single, locked workflow: the founder uploads a contract, the system analyses it, risky or uncertain findings are routed to a human, and the founder exports a report. A conversational assistant extends the workflow to drafting without replacing it.

1.6 Goals of the Project

  1. Make contract risk legible to a non-lawyer. Every finding must state, in plain English, what the clause does, why it matters to a startup specifically, and what a founder might reasonably negotiate.
  2. Detect what is missing, not merely what is present. Absent protections are the most dangerous category and the hardest to notice unaided.
  3. Fail safely and visibly. The product must degrade gracefully when the model fails, and must never silently discard a finding.
  4. Refuse to give legal advice. Enforced structurally, not by convention.
  5. Keep a human in the loop where it matters. Uncertain, severe, or suspicious findings must not be auto-approved.
  6. Be auditable. Every consequential action must leave a tamper-evident record.

1.7 Scope and Exclusions

In scope: PDF and DOCX ingestion; a fixed taxonomy of ten clause types; per-clause risk scoring; whole-document loophole and ambiguity sweeps; a review queue with role-based access control; JSON and PDF export; an audit ledger; a conversational assistant with document drafting; an administrative console.

Explicitly excluded, recorded as scope locks before implementation began: enterprise single sign-on and SAML; multi-region deployment; SOC 2, HIPAA, or ISO 27001 certification; marketplace integrations; multi-language contracts; automated legal approval; billing automation; and — the most consequential exclusion — custom model training or fine-tuning. The system consumes a hosted model through a provider abstraction. This is Decision D8, defended in Section 8.4 rather than concealed.

1.8 Ethical Considerations

Three ethical commitments shaped the engineering, and each is enforced in code rather than asserted in prose.

The system must not practise law. Every AI-generated response carries a machine-enforced not_legal_advice flag together with a canonical disclaimer. These are part of the response schema; a response lacking them fails validation and is never returned to the user. Jurisdiction-specific questions are refused outright and redirected to licensed counsel.

The system must not conceal its uncertainty. Confidence is surfaced, and low confidence carries a consequence: the finding is routed to a human. A system that hides doubt in order to appear authoritative is, in this domain, dangerous.

The system must respect erasure. A user who deletes their account has every artefact they own removed from the database. The single exception — the tamper-evident audit chain, which stores an actor and an action but never document content — is retained deliberately and defended in Section 8.3.

1.9 Significance

The significance of this work is not that it applies a language model to contracts; several commercial products do. It is that it treats the language model as an untrusted, unreliable, adversarially-reachable component and constructs the compensating controls that make it safe to deploy. Graceful degradation to a deterministic backstop, injection fencing, confidence-gated human review, and tamper-evident logging generalise to any system that places a probabilistic model in a position of consequence. That is the transferable contribution.

Chapter 2

Existing System

2.1 Related Work

Contract review software is a mature category, but almost all of it is addressed to a professional audience. Enterprise contract-lifecycle-management platforms treat review as one stage in a procurement pipeline and assume an in-house legal team. Clause-library products assist lawyers in assembling documents from approved fragments. Recent entrants add a language model to summarise or compare clauses.

Two observations motivated our design. First, none of these tools is addressed to a person who has never read a term sheet before; they explain what a clause says rather than what it will do to you. Second, and more importantly for an engineering thesis, published tools rarely make explicit what happens when the model fails or is attacked. The literature on prompt injection establishes that any system feeding untrusted text to a language model is exposed to instruction hijacking. A contract is, definitionally, untrusted text supplied by an adverse party. Yet the failure semantics of contract-analysis tools — what they do when the model is unavailable, or when it has been manipulated — are seldom stated. We therefore treated failure behaviour, rather than raw analytical accuracy, as the axis on which to compete.

2.3 Novelty

The novelty claimed by this project is deliberately narrow and defensible. We do not claim a novel model, a novel training method, or superior raw accuracy. We claim a novel safety envelope for a rented model deployed in a consequential domain, comprising five mechanisms that are, to our knowledge, not found together in any comparable student or commercial system:

  1. Graceful degradation to a deterministic backstop. A three-link provider chain terminating in a rules engine that requires no network and no model, so that analysis always returns a result.
  2. Adversarial-input handling for the document itself. The contract is fenced as untrusted data, break-out sequences are neutralised, and suspected injection forces human review.
  3. Confidence-gated human-in-the-loop routing. Uncertainty has a mechanical consequence rather than a cosmetic one.
  4. Tamper-evident audit chaining. Consequential actions are recorded such that retrospective alteration is detectable, and this is proven by a test that performs the alteration.
  5. Schema-enforced non-advice. The disclaimer is a validated field of the response type; a response without it cannot be serialised and therefore cannot be returned.

2.4 Solution Available in Clarifyd

Clarifyd delivers the workflow of Figure 1.1 as a deployed web application. A founder signs in, uploads a contract, and receives a ranked list of findings, each carrying a severity, a risk score, a confidence, a plain-English explanation of the business consequence, and a suggested negotiating position. A health gauge summarises the document. A loophole sweep lists protections that should be present and are not. An ambiguity sweep lists terms that are undefined or could later be argued either way. Findings that are severe, low-confidence, or suspected of manipulation appear in a reviewer's queue rather than being silently accepted. The founder may export a report, and every export is recorded in the audit ledger. A conversational assistant answers follow-up questions and can draft a document clause by clause, refusing to produce a draft until every essential term has been supplied.

2.5 Use Case Diagrams

Three actors interact with the system. The Founder is the primary actor and owns contracts. The Reviewer adjudicates findings that the system has refused to auto-approve. The Administrator manages accounts and verifies the integrity of the audit chain. A fourth, non-human actor — the Reasoning Provider — is external and untrusted, and is shown because its failure is a modelled case rather than an exception.

Chapter 3

Software Process Model

3.1 Candidate Process Models

Three models were considered. The Waterfall model was rejected because the behaviour of a hosted language model cannot be specified in advance; the prompt, the rubric, and the confidence threshold were all discovered empirically, and a process that forbids revisiting an earlier phase would have made that discovery impossible. Pure Scrum was rejected for the opposite reason: a strict two-week sprint cadence with a negotiable backlog invites the frontend to begin before the API contract is stable, and the resulting rework is precisely the failure mode that sinks small student teams. The Incremental model with formal gates was chosen, and Agile practices (short iterations, continuous integration, a living backlog) were adopted inside each increment.

3.2 The Proposed Model

The process actually followed was backend-first, gated, incremental development. Its defining rule was recorded in writing before implementation began:

The gating rule

The API request and response schemas are frozen at task T1. Frontend implementation may not begin until the backend passes a readiness checkpoint. After the freeze, schema changes must be additive only and require explicit sign-off. The freeze is recorded in the code itself as API_CONTRACT_VERSION.

Two further decisions were recorded at the same time and are referenced throughout this report. Decision D1 (SLC over MVP): complete one workflow lovably rather than ship several partially. Decision D8 (no model training): consume a hosted model through a provider abstraction; do not train or fine-tune.

3.3 Why a Gated Incremental Model was Chosen

The gate exists to protect the smaller, more junior half of the work from the larger, more volatile half. In a four-person team where two members depend on an API that two other members are still discovering, an unfrozen contract means the dependent work is rebuilt every week. Freezing the schemas early cost us some flexibility — two desirable response fields had to be added additively rather than designed in — and bought us a frontend that never had to be rewritten. On a two-week critical path, that trade is decisively correct.

The incremental structure also suited the nature of the AI work, which is empirical rather than specifiable. The scoring rubric went through four revisions, each validated against a hand-labelled corpus. That is discovery, and it needs iteration; but it happened behind a stable interface, so it never destabilised anything downstream.

Chapter 4

Proposed System

4.1 Need for the Proposed System

Chapter 2 established that no existing system serves a non-lawyer founder with an explanation of business consequence, and that the systems which come closest — general-purpose chatbots — are precisely the ones with no defined failure behaviour, no defence against a hostile document, and no restraint on giving legal advice. The proposed system fills that gap: the accessibility of a chatbot, with the failure discipline of production infrastructure.

4.2 Features of the Proposed System

Contract ingestion and gating
PDF and DOCX accepted, validated by content rather than filename, deduplicated by content hash, and rejected outright if the document is not a contract.
Clause segmentation and risk scoring
Ten clause types, deterministic segmentation, and per-clause scoring by the language model with eight clauses evaluated concurrently.
Whole-document sweeps
A narrative report, a loophole sweep that reports missing protections as well as present ones, and an ambiguity sweep with remediation suggestions.
Confidence-gated human review
Findings that are low-confidence, severe, or suspected of manipulation are routed to a reviewer and cannot be auto-approved.
Clarifyd AI assistant
Answers follow-up questions, refuses jurisdiction-specific legal opinions, and drafts documents clause by clause — withholding the draft until every essential term has been supplied.
Export and audit
JSON and PDF export, with every consequential action appended to a tamper-evident hash chain.
Administration and erasure
An administrative console with a full data purge on account deletion, reachable from both deletion paths (Section 8.3).

4.3 Business Context

The user is a pre-seed founder; the buyer is the same person, paying from a limited personal or company budget. The competitive substitute is not another product — it is doing nothing and signing anyway, which is what most founders currently do. The product therefore competes on trust and speed, not on feature count. Clarifyd is positioned explicitly as decision-support that helps a founder arrive at counsel better prepared, or negotiate small matters without counsel at all. It is not positioned as a replacement for a lawyer, and the software refuses to behave as one.

4.4 Operating Environment

  • Client. Any current desktop or mobile browser. No installation.
  • Server. A Python application served over HTTPS on a managed cloud platform, deployed automatically from the main branch.
  • Database. PostgreSQL in production (managed, serverless); SQLite in development, so a new team member can run the system with no database installation.
  • Model provider. A hosted Llama-3.1-70B model reached over an OpenAI-compatible interface, with a second model and a local rules engine behind it.
  • Identity. A specialist authentication provider holds credentials. The system never stores a password.
Chapter 5

System Design

5.1 Brief Description

Clarifyd is a three-tier system: a browser client, an application server, and an external reasoning provider. The server is layered strictly — routes validate and delegate, services hold business logic and import no web framework, and models hold persistence. This separation is what allows the entire reasoning stack to be replaced (or removed entirely, falling back to rules) without touching a single route.

5.2 UI/UX Design

The interface follows a single design system in which every colour, spacing step, and type size is defined once as a token and reused. Three decisions are worth recording because each solves a specific problem.

Analysis survives navigation. A cold analysis takes up to twenty seconds. Analysis state is held above the page outlet, so a founder who navigates away and back finds the work still running, with a progress indicator that follows them.

Authentication code is kept off public pages. The authentication library is roughly 220 KB. Marketing pages need no session, so they render under a credential-free stub provider and never download it.

Browser-stored data is namespaced by account identifier. Results cached in the browser are keyed by the user's permanent account id — never their email address — and swept on logout or account switch. The reason this is a design principle rather than an implementation detail is given in Section 8.3.

5.9 The Reasoning Engine and its Safety Controls

This section documents the mechanisms on which the novelty claim of Section 2.3 rests.

5.9.1 The fallback chain

5.9.2 Prompt-injection defence

The contract is supplied by the counterparty and is therefore treated as hostile input. Three controls apply. First, clause text is fenced as untrusted data and the system prompt is instructed that nothing within the fence is a directive. Second, sequences that attempt to break out of the fence — forged closing tags, role markers — are neutralised. Third, a heuristic pre-check sets an injection_suspected flag; a flagged finding is force-routed to human review regardless of its confidence. The defence is validated by tests that submit deliberately malicious contracts (Section 7.6).

5.9.3 The confidence gate

A finding is routed to a human when any of the following holds: confidence < 0.7; severity is high or critical; or injection is suspected. The disjunction matters. A confident, critical finding is still reviewed, because confidence and correctness are not the same thing and the cost of a false negative here is catastrophic.

5.9.4 The tamper-evident audit chain

5.9.5 Schema-enforced non-advice

Every reasoning response type declares not_legal_advice as a constant-true field together with a canonical disclaimer string. Because this is part of the validated response schema, a response constructed without it fails validation and is never serialised. The guarantee is therefore structural: it cannot be forgotten by a developer, and it cannot be stripped by a caller.

6.2 The Analysis Pipeline in Code

The pipeline of Figure 5.6 is implemented as eight stages, each independently testable.

  1. Upload validation. Filename canonicalisation, extension allowlist, magic-byte inspection (%PDF-, PK\x03\x04), a 25 MB cap, and a SHA-256 content hash used for deduplication.
  2. Text extraction. PDF via pypdf; DOCX by reading the underlying XML directly. Empty text is a rejection, not an empty analysis.
  3. Contract gate. A density heuristic over contract vocabulary, with a single fast model call reserved for genuinely ambiguous documents.
  4. Clause segmentation. Sentence splitting, heading fusion, continuation merging, and classification into ten types by keyword lexicon — deterministic by design (FR-04).
  5. Per-clause scoring. Eight concurrent calls through the provider chain, each returning severity, score, confidence and rationale, cached by (provider, model, sha256(text)).
  6. Whole-document sweeps. Three concurrent passes: narrative report, loophole sweep (including absent clauses), ambiguity sweep.
  7. Ranking. Ordered by (severity, score, confidence), asserted by test so the order can never silently change.
  8. Persistence, routing, audit. Findings stored; the confidence gate applied; an audit event appended to the chain.

6.3 Engineering Problems Encountered and Solved

Four problems are recorded here because each changed the design, and each is the kind of problem that does not appear in a tutorial.

6.3.1 The model was withdrawn mid-project

The primary model (Kimi K2) was retired by the provider from our account partway through development. Because the system speaks a generic wire protocol behind a provider interface (Figure 5.7), migrating to Llama-3.1-70B was a configuration change with zero code modification. Had we adopted a vendor SDK, this would have been a rewrite. This is the clearest empirical vindication of an architectural decision in the project (NFR-12).

6.3.2 Findings were being silently dropped

On long contracts the provider began rate-limiting us, and the failing calls were being discarded. The founder would have received a quietly incomplete analysis — the worst possible failure mode for this product, because it is invisible. We introduced a shared token-bucket limiter that paces all concurrent calls. Throttling now increases latency rather than reducing completeness. Slower is acceptable; silently wrong is not.

6.3.3 The serverless event loop

On the serverless platform, application startup hooks did not fire as they do on a persistent server, and an HTTP client bound to a closed event loop produced intermittent, hard-to-reproduce failures. The fix was to build the client lazily per request and detect a new loop rather than assume process-level initialisation. This is a genuine distributed-systems defect, and diagnosing it required reasoning about the platform's execution model rather than reading our own code.

6.3.4 A privacy defect in our own product

Late in the project we discovered that a deleted user's data reappeared when a new account was created on the same email address. The diagnosis, the fix, and — importantly — the reasoning error we made while investigating, are documented as a case study in Section 8.3.

7.2 Testing a Non-Deterministic System

The central testing problem

A language model returns different text on every call. It cannot be asserted against a fixed expected string. The resolution adopted here is to separate behaviour from quality:

Behaviour is deterministic and is tested strictly. The response must satisfy the schema. The findings must be ordered by (severity, score, confidence). The disclaimer must be present. Confidence below the threshold must produce a queue entry. If the primary provider raises, the fallback must be invoked, and if all providers fail, the rules engine must still return a result. These are yes-or-no properties and are asserted in ordinary unit tests with the provider replaced by a stub.

Quality is statistical and is measured separately. A benchmark harness runs the live model against a hand-labelled corpus of contracts and reports agreement with our own severity labels. This runs outside the pull-request gate, because it is slow, costly, and non-deterministic. Reported result: approximately 89% exact-band agreement, with 100% of findings within one band, and no false-critical findings on benign contracts.

In short: we do not test the model's prose. We test the envelope around it.

7.3 Black-Box Testing

Black-box (behavioural) testing examines the system through its public interface without reference to internal structure: inputs are supplied, outputs are compared against the specification. It is the appropriate technique for the requirement shells of Table 2.2, since each shell carries a fit criterion expressed in terms of observable behaviour. Its advantages are that tests are written from the requirement rather than the implementation, remain valid across refactoring, and can be written by someone who did not write the code — which, in our team, was deliberately the case.

7.5 White-Box Testing

White-box testing exercises internal paths with knowledge of the implementation. It was applied where the interesting behaviour is a branch rather than an output. The provider chain is the clearest example: the fallback path cannot be reached from outside without taking the real provider offline, so the provider is replaced by a stub that raises on demand, and the tests assert that the second provider is invoked, that the rules engine is invoked when the second also fails, and that a schema-invalid response is treated as a failure rather than accepted. Similarly, cache hit and miss paths, retry-exhaustion, and the atomic-claim race are all exercised structurally.

7.6 Security Testing

Four security properties are tested as first-class requirements rather than reviewed by inspection.

  • Upload safety (14 tests). Disguised content, oversized payloads, and traversal-shaped filenames.
  • Prompt injection. Deliberately malicious contracts are submitted; the assertion is that the injection is detected and the finding is force-routed to a human (TC-16).
  • Authorisation (SSRF, IDOR). Internal addresses are rejected; cross-user object access is denied server-side (TC-21, TC-22).
  • Audit integrity. The tamper test (TC-19) mutates a historical record and asserts detection. This is the test we are most confident in, because it attacks the system rather than confirming it.

8.2 Deployment Architecture

8.3 A Case Study: A Privacy Defect Found and Fixed

This section is included because the defect is instructive, because the reasoning error we made while diagnosing it is more instructive still, and because a report that presents only successes is not an engineering document.

8.3.1 The symptom

An account was deleted from the administrative console. A new account was then created using the same email address. The new account was not new: it presented the deleted user's contracts, their prior analyses, and their saved assistant conversation.

8.3.2 The wrong hypothesis — and why it mattered

Our first hypothesis was that the server was failing to delete the data and was re-associating it with the new account. That hypothesis was wrong, and pursuing it would have led us to fix a defect that did not exist. The data had never been on the server after deletion. It had never left the browser.

8.3.3 The actual cause

Results cached in the browser were namespaced by the user's email address. An email address is reusable: it is a label a person currently holds, and it can be reassigned. When the account was recreated on the same address, the namespace matched the residue left in that browser, and the application served it to the new account.

A second instance of the same category error was then found on the server: the one-time sign-up code table is keyed by email address, so a recreated account could inherit pending verification codes belonging to the deleted user. The root cause was identical — a reassignable attribute used as an identity key.

8.3.4 The fix, and its scope

Browser-stored data is now namespaced by the identity provider's account identifier, which is permanent and never reused; all namespaced entries are swept on logout and on account switch. Server-side, account deletion now routes through a single purge function shared by both deletion paths (Figure 5.5), which removes every row the account owns — including the email-keyed verification rows.

We state the blast radius precisely rather than dramatically: the exposure was limited to the same physical browser. A different person registering that email address on their own device would have seen nothing, because the residue was local to the machine. It was a shared-device leak, not a server-side disclosure. Overstating it would be as dishonest as concealing it.

8.3.5 Why the audit chain is retained on deletion

The purge deliberately does not delete AuditEvent rows. Removing links from a hash chain destroys the property that makes it valuable: the ability to prove that nothing has been altered. The chain records an actor and an action — never contract content and never personal detail. The position taken, and defended, is that erasure covers personal data and content; it does not require the destruction of a tamper-evident record that an event occurred.

The generalisable lesson

An email address is not an identity. It is a label a principal currently holds, and it can be reassigned to another principal. Private data must never be keyed on any attribute that can be reassigned. This single principle explains both instances of the defect, and it is the transferable result of the case study.

8.4 Limitations

Stated before an examiner finds them, and each as a decision with a reason rather than an omission.

  1. No model was trained. Deliberate (Decision D8). Training a competitive model requires data and compute beyond the project, and the contribution claimed here is the safety envelope, not the model.
  2. Clause classification is a keyword lexicon, not machine learning. Chosen for determinism, zero latency, and zero cost. The risk judgement — the part that genuinely requires intelligence — is the model's.
  3. Guidance text in two reasoning routes is a deterministic scaffold. The live model path runs through the analysis pipeline and the assistant, not through those two endpoints. Stated plainly rather than implied otherwise.
  4. The frontend has no unit tests. Type-checking is its only automated gate. This is a genuine gap, not a design choice.
  5. Two features are API-only. Compare and compliance have endpoints but no interface.
  6. The second backend is unfinished and undeployed. Presented as forward-looking architecture, and claimed as nothing more.
  7. Confidence is a routing threshold, not a calibrated probability. It determines what a human must inspect. We do not claim that 0.7 corresponds to a 70% likelihood of correctness; establishing that would require calibration work we have not performed, and asserting it would be an overclaim.
  8. A single model gateway is used in production. The abstraction supports others, and the mid-project migration (Section 6.3.1) demonstrated that it works — but only one is configured today.

8.5 Future Work

  1. Wire the scaffolded reasoning routes to the live model path, closing limitation 3.
  2. Replace the keyword lexicon with a learned classifier using embeddings, and measure whether the loss of determinism is repaid in accuracy.
  3. Calibrate the confidence signal against outcomes, so the threshold can be defended statistically rather than pragmatically.
  4. Add frontend unit and end-to-end tests, closing limitation 4.
  5. Promote the second backend (retrieval-augmented library search, deadline monitoring, counsel handoff) and retire the two-backend split.
  6. Build the missing compare and compliance interfaces.
  7. Retrospectively purge rows orphaned by deletions performed before the fix of Section 8.3 — the purge governs deletions from that point forward, and does not clean historical residue.
Conclusion

Conclusion

Clarifyd set out to make the risk hidden in a startup contract legible to the person who must sign it. The delivered system does that: it ingests a contract, decides whether it is a contract at all, segments it, scores each clause for risk and confidence, sweeps the whole document for loopholes — including protections that are dangerously absent — and explains each finding in plain English with its consequence for a startup.

The engineering contribution, however, is not the analysis. It is the discipline around it. A language model is a rented, probabilistic component that fails silently, can be manipulated by the very document it is asked to read, and may be withdrawn by its provider without notice. Every one of those things happened during this project. The system answers each with a mechanism that is implemented, tested, and defensible: a fallback chain that terminates in a deterministic engine requiring no network, so the product never hard-fails; an injection defence that treats the contract as the hostile input it genuinely is; a confidence gate that gives uncertainty a mechanical consequence rather than a cosmetic one; a hash-chained audit log that makes retrospective alteration detectable and locatable; and a non-legal-advice guarantee enforced by schema validation, so that it cannot be forgotten or stripped.

We also found a real privacy defect in our own product, diagnosed it — correcting a wrong hypothesis along the way — fixed its root cause in two layers, and reported its true blast radius without exaggeration. We regard that episode, and the honest limitations recorded in Section 8.4, as part of the result rather than a blemish upon it.

The transferable claim of this thesis is narrow and, we believe, sound: when a probabilistic model is placed in a position of consequence, the engineering that matters is not the model. It is the envelope that makes the model safe to depend upon.
