Part 2 — Project basics (every member must know these)

What is Clarifyd? (in one breath)

In simple words

A contract is a legal agreement — for example, the paperwork an investor gives a young company when they invest money in it. These documents are long, written in difficult language, and one bad sentence buried on page nine can cost the business owner everything they own.

Our users are founders — people starting a small company. They usually cannot afford a lawyer, and lawyers are slow. So founders often sign these documents without truly understanding them.

Clarifyd is a website. The founder uploads their contract. Our system reads it, finds the dangerous parts, and explains each one in normal everyday English — like a knowledgeable friend, not a lawyer.

Say this

“Clarifyd is an AI contract risk analyzer for pre-seed startup founders. A founder uploads a SAFE, an NDA or a term sheet. We extract the clauses, score each one for risk and confidence, sweep the document for loopholes — including protections that are missing — and explain every finding in plain English. It is decision-support. It is never legal advice.”

The user's journey (memorise this chain)

The eight-step pipeline (know the order)

1. Validate the file (read its real bytes) → 2. Extract the text → 3. Is it even a contract? → 4. Split into clauses → 5. Score each clause with the AI (8 at a time) → 6. Three whole-document sweeps → 7. Rank the findings → 8. Save, route risky ones to a human, write an audit record.

Memory trick: Validate, Extract, Gate, Split, Score, Sweep, Rank, Record — “Very Easy Girls Sing Sweet Songs, Rank Records.”

The four safety controls (the heart of the project — ANY member may be asked)

ControlWhat it means in one sentence
Fallback chainMain AI → second AI → a plain rules engine that needs no internet. The product never fails completely.
Injection defenceThe contract was written by the other side and may contain hidden instructions aimed at our AI. We treat it as hostile.

Plus: every AI answer carries a “not legal advice” label that cannot be removed — strip it and the response fails validation and never reaches the user.

Part 3 — Topic 1: The backend, and why it is layered

In simple words

The backend is the part of a website you never see. When you press a button, your browser sends a message over the internet to a program running on an always-on computer somewhere. That program does the real work — reads the file, talks to the AI, saves things in the database — and sends an answer back.

I built ours in three strict layers, and the rule is that a layer may only talk to the one below it:

  • Routes — the front door. They check who you are and whether your request is valid, then hand the work downwards. They contain no thinking.
  • Services — the brain. All the actual logic lives here: analysing a contract, guarding against attacks, writing audit records. These files do not even know that a website exists.
  • Database layer — storage. Nothing else.
Say this

“The backend is layered strictly. Routes validate and delegate but contain no logic. Services hold all the business logic and deliberately import no web framework at all. The database layer only stores. That separation is not decoration — it is what let us replace the entire AI provider without touching a single route.”

If pushed: Why does that separation actually matter? Give me a concrete benefit.

“Mid-project our AI provider withdrew the model we were using. Because the services depend on an interface rather than on a specific model, swapping to a different model was a configuration change with zero code modification. If our logic had been mixed into the routes, that would have been a rewrite. The layering paid for itself in a real emergency.”

Part 4 — Topic 2: The database (15 tables)

In simple words

A database is where information is kept permanently, so it survives when the program restarts. Think of it as a set of spreadsheets. Each sheet is called a table. We have fifteen.

The three that matter most form what I call the spine: a User owns Contracts; each contract has Findings (the individual risks we discovered inside it). Everything else hangs off that backbone.

The one question you will definitely be asked here

Two reviewers open the queue at the same instant and both click the same item. What stops them both getting it?

“We do not check-then-update, because both reviewers could pass the check in the same moment and both proceed. Instead we issue a single update that says: set the assignee to me, but only on the condition that this row is still unclaimed. The database guarantees that only one of those two updates can win. The loser gets a conflict response and the interface refreshes. The safety comes from the database, not from our code being lucky with timing.”

Why this matters

This is a race condition, and it is a classic examiner question because most students get it wrong. Knowing that the check-then-act pattern is broken — and that the fix is a conditional update — is the difference between a student who copied a tutorial and one who understands concurrency.

Why caches exist (and why they are a correctness feature, not just speed)

Say this

“The cache tables are keyed by a fingerprint of the contract's own content. So if the same contract is uploaded twice, the second analysis is instant and free. But the deeper reason is reproducibility: identical input gives byte-identical output. That matters when you have an audit trail to defend — a finding must not change underneath you.”

Part 5 — Topic 3: Security (four attacks, and how we closed them)

Attack 1 — a disguised file upload

In simple words

Anyone can take a harmful file and simply rename it to contract.pdf. The name proves nothing, because the attacker chooses the name. Trusting a filename is like believing a stranger's claim about their own identity with no ID.

So we ignore the name entirely and open the file to read its first few bytes. Every genuine PDF begins with the characters %PDF-. Every Word file begins with PK (because a .docx is secretly a zip archive). These opening bytes are called magic bytes. If they are wrong, we reject the file, whatever it claims to be called.

Say this

“We check the file's actual bytes, not its extension — because the attacker controls the filename. A real PDF starts with the characters %PDF. If those bytes are missing, we reject it. We have fourteen tests on upload security alone.”

Attack 2 — SSRF (making our own server attack itself)

In simple words

We let a user analyse a contract that lives at a web address. But a web address can point inwards — at our own private servers, or at the cloud provider's internal control panel, which normally nobody outside can reach.

An attacker who can make our server fetch their chosen address is using our server as a weapon against ourselves. That attack is called Server-Side Request Forgery. We block it: no redirections, and only normal public web ports are allowed.

Attack 3 — IDOR (reading someone else's contract by guessing)

In simple words

Suppose your contract is number 41 in our system. What stops you from typing 42 into the address bar and reading somebody else's contract? On badly built websites, nothing does — they merely hide the button in the interface, which stops nobody.

This is called Insecure Direct Object Reference. We check ownership on the server, on every single request. Hiding a button is not security.

Say this

“If my contract is number 41, I must not be able to type 42 and read yours. We check ownership on the server for every draft and every export. Hiding the button in the interface is not security — it only hides the door, it does not lock it.”

Attack 4 — leaked secrets

In simple words

Passwords and API keys get accidentally saved into the code, where anybody who can see the code can read them. This happens constantly in real projects. We run an automatic scanner on every change that catches keys before they are committed, and after one audit we rotated every credential as a precaution.

Part 6 — Topic 4: The tamper-evident audit chain (your strongest topic)

In simple words

Every important action — a contract analysed, a report exported, a user deleted — is written into a history log.

The clever part is how we write it. First, understand a hash: it is a short fingerprint calculated from some data. Change even one letter of the data, and the fingerprint comes out completely different. You cannot work backwards from the fingerprint to the data. We use one called SHA-256.

Now the trick: each record's fingerprint is calculated from its own data plus the fingerprint of the record before it. The records are chained together, like links.

So if someone quietly edits an old record, that record's fingerprint changes — but the next record still stores the old fingerprint. They no longer match. The chain is visibly broken, and we can point to exactly where.

Trap — be careful here

The examiner will almost certainly ask: “Couldn't an administrator just edit the database directly?”

Do NOT say the database cannot be changed. That is false, and a sharp examiner will destroy you for it.

Say this

“Yes, they can. And that is precisely the point — they cannot do it quietly. The chain does not prevent tampering; it makes tampering detectable, and it tells us exactly which record was touched. We prove this with a test that deliberately corrupts a record in the middle of the chain and asserts that our verification endpoint reports the break.”

Why this matters

That distinction — prevents versus detects — is the whole point of the technique. A student who says ‘it makes the data unchangeable’ does not understand what a hash chain is. A student who says ‘it makes changes detectable’ clearly does. This single sentence can move your grade.

Part 7 — Topic 5: Account deletion (does deleted mean deleted?)

In simple words

If somebody asks us to delete their account, everything of theirs should genuinely disappear. That is both an ethical duty and a legal one (the ‘right to erasure’).

We found that it did not. Deleting an account removed the user and their contracts — but quietly left behind their uploaded letterhead image, their comments, their feedback, their contact messages, and their unused sign-up codes.

There was a second, worse problem. An account can be deleted in two places: on our own admin screen, or in the dashboard of Clerk (the login company we use). Deleting it in Clerk's dashboard removed the account from Clerk — and left all their data in our database, orphaned and unreachable.

Say this

“Both ways of deleting an account now call the same erase function, so they cannot drift apart. For the Clerk path we added a webhook — a message Clerk sends us the instant a user is deleted. Because that message tells our server to erase somebody's data, we verify it is genuinely from Clerk using a cryptographic signature. If the signature is missing, wrong, or the message is old, we refuse it. And if the signing key is not configured at all, the endpoint refuses everything — otherwise anyone who found that web address could delete our users.”

Trap — be careful here

Expect this challenge: “You keep the audit log after deletion. Isn't that a contradiction of the right to erasure?” It is a sharp question. Welcome it.

Say this

“The audit log holds an action and an actor — ‘user X exported a report at 2pm’. It never holds contract content or personal details. So we erase the person's content and personal data, and we retain an integrity ledger. Erasure covers personal data; it does not require destroying the tamper-proof record that an event happened. That is the position real systems take, and we can defend it.”

Part 8 — The technologies you must be able to explain

For each one the examiner may ask: what is it, why did you use it, and what else could you have used? These are the answers.

FastAPIthe web framework · Python
What it is (plain words)
A web framework is a toolkit that lets a program listen for requests from the internet and answer them. FastAPI is the one we used, written in Python.
Where it came from
Created by Sebastián Ramírez in 2018. It became popular very quickly because it does two things at once: it is asynchronous (it can wait for many slow things at the same time), and it validates data automatically using Python's type hints.
Why we chose it
Our work is waiting-heavy: for one contract we send eight questions to the AI and wait for all of them. An asynchronous framework handles that naturally — while one request waits, the server serves others. A traditional framework would need one worker blocked per request.
What we rejected, and why
Django was rejected: it is excellent but heavier, and its strengths (admin panels, its own ORM conventions) were not what we needed. Flask was rejected: simpler, but has no built-in data validation, and we depend on validation for a safety guarantee (see Pydantic below).
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every endpoint: /analyze/contract, /reviews, /admin/users, /webhooks/clerk.
Pydanticdata validation — and our safety guarantee
What it is (plain words)
A library that checks that data has the right shape. You describe what a valid response looks like, and it refuses anything that does not match.
Where it came from
Created by Samuel Colvin in 2017. Version 2 rewrote its core in Rust, making it extremely fast. It is the validation engine that FastAPI is built on.
Why we chose it
This is the part most students miss, so say it clearly: our “not legal advice” guarantee is implemented as data validation. Every AI response type declares that the disclaimer field must be present and must be true. If any programmer ever forgot it, or tried to strip it, the response would fail validation and never be sent. The safety property is enforced by the same machinery that parses the data.
What we rejected, and why
Hand-written checks were rejected — a human can forget to call a check. A schema cannot be forgotten, because nothing gets out without passing through it.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every request and response shape; the frozen API contract; the enforced disclaimer.
PostgreSQL & SQLitethe database
What it is (plain words)
SQLite is a database that lives in a single file on your computer — zero setup. PostgreSQL is a full database server, used by very large systems.
Where it came from
SQLite was written by D. Richard Hipp in 2000 and is now the most widely deployed database in the world — it is inside every phone. PostgreSQL began at the University of California, Berkeley in 1986 and is the reference-standard open-source database.
Why we chose it
We use SQLite in development so a new team member can run the whole system with no database to install, and PostgreSQL in production where we need a real server, concurrent users, and managed backups. Our code is written against an abstraction, so the same code runs on both.
What we rejected, and why
MongoDB was rejected. Our data is highly relational — a user owns contracts, a contract has findings, a finding may have a review action. That is exactly what relational databases are for. Choosing a document store for relational data creates work rather than saving it.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
All fifteen tables; migrations keep the two engines in step.
Clerkauthentication — who the user is
What it is (plain words)
Authentication means proving who somebody is — the login. Clerk is a specialist company that does logins for you: sign-up, passwords, Google sign-in, sessions, password reset.
Where it came from
Founded in 2020 (Colin Sidoti and Braden Sidoti). It belongs to a category of services — alongside Auth0 and Firebase Authentication — that exist because almost everybody who builds their own login gets it wrong, and the consequences are severe.
Why we chose it
We deliberately chose never to store a password at all. If we do not hold passwords, we cannot leak them. That removes an entire category of vulnerability from our project, and it removes it by design, not by us being careful. Clerk also gives us permanent, never-reused account identifiers — which turned out to matter enormously (Part 9).
What we rejected, and why
Rolling our own was rejected: password hashing, session management, token expiry, reset flows and social login are each easy to get subtly wrong, and a student project getting it wrong means leaking real people's credentials. Auth0 was a fair alternative; Clerk had the better developer experience for Next.js.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every login; every request carries a short-lived signed token which our backend verifies. Clerk also notifies us via a signed webhook when a user is deleted in its dashboard.
JWT & JWKShow the server knows the token is real
What it is (plain words)
A JWT (JSON Web Token) is a small piece of text the browser sends with every request that says ‘I am user X, and this was issued at 10:31’. It is digitally signed, so it cannot be forged. JWKS is the public list of keys used to check those signatures.
Where it came from
The JWT standard was published in 2015 (RFC 7519). Signing uses public-key cryptography: Clerk signs with a private key that only Clerk holds, and anybody can verify with the matching public key.
Why we chose it
Our backend never has to phone Clerk to ask ‘is this user real?’ on every request — that would be slow and would fail if Clerk were briefly down. Instead we verify the signature ourselves using the public key. Tokens expire after about a minute, so a stolen token is almost worthless.
What we rejected, and why
Storing a session in our own database was rejected: it means a database read on every single request, and it means we hold the session state.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every authenticated request. The verification code lives in our auth layer.
SHA-256the fingerprint behind the audit chain
What it is (plain words)
A hash function turns any amount of data into a short fixed-length fingerprint. Change one character of the input and the fingerprint changes completely. You cannot reverse it to recover the input.
Where it came from
SHA-256 was designed by the United States National Security Agency and published by NIST in 2001 as part of the SHA-2 family (standard FIPS 180-4). It is the same function that secures Bitcoin. It is considered secure today; its predecessor SHA-1 is broken and must not be used.
Why we chose it
It gives us tamper-evidence for the audit log (Part 6), and it gives us content-addressed caching — the same contract always produces the same fingerprint, so we can recognise a re-upload instantly.
What we rejected, and why
MD5 and SHA-1 were rejected because both are cryptographically broken — an attacker can construct two different inputs with the same fingerprint, which would let them forge an audit record.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
The audit chain; contract deduplication; the cache keys.
Docker & DigitalOceanhow it gets on the internet
What it is (plain words)
Docker packages an application together with everything it needs to run, so it behaves identically on your laptop and on the server. DigitalOcean App Platform runs that package on the internet for us.
Where it came from
Docker appeared in 2013 and largely ended the ‘but it works on my machine’ problem by making the environment part of the deliverable.
Why we chose it
Every push to our main branch automatically rebuilds and redeploys. That means the deployed system is always the reviewed system — there is no manual step where somebody forgets to upload the newest version.
What we rejected, and why
Manual deployment (copying files to a server) was rejected as error-prone and unrepeatable.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
The live backend and frontend; the deployment configuration is committed to the repository.

Part 9 — Your bug story (volunteer this — it wins marks)

In simple words

We deleted a test user. Then we signed up again with the same email address. The ‘new’ account was not new at all — it still had the old user's contracts, results, and even their old chat conversation. A deleted person had come back from the dead.

Our first instinct was that the server was leaking deleted data. That instinct was wrong, and if we had chased it we would have fixed the wrong thing entirely.

The truth: browsers can store data on your own computer. We were storing each person's results in the browser, labelled with their email address. But an email address can be reused. Delete the account, sign up again with the same email, and the label matches the data still sitting in that browser. So the app handed the old data to the new account. Nothing had ever left the browser.

Then I found the same mistake in the database: our sign-up code table is keyed by email address too, so a recreated account could inherit the deleted user's pending codes. Same root cause, different layer.

Say this

“An email address is not an identity. It is a label a person currently holds, and it can be reassigned to somebody else. An account ID is permanent and is never reused. We were keying private data on the reassignable thing. Now we key it on the permanent thing. And we found the same error in a second layer, which tells you it was a conceptual mistake, not a typo.”

Trap — be careful here

State the blast radius honestly. Do not exaggerate it, and do not minimise it.

It affected the same physical browser only. A stranger signing up with that email on their own laptop saw nothing, because the leftover data was on that one machine. It was a shared-device leak, not a server disclosure. Examiners respect precision far more than drama.

Part 10 — Question bank (cover the answers and test yourself)

Walk me through what happens when a founder uploads a contract.

“Eight steps. We validate the file by reading its real opening bytes. We extract the text. We check it is actually a contract. We split it into clauses. We score each clause with the AI, eight at a time. We run three sweeps over the whole document, including a hunt for missing protections. We rank the findings. Then we save them, push anything risky to a human reviewer, and write a tamper-proof audit record.”

Why is your backend layered? Isn't that over-engineering for a student project?

“It earned its keep in a real emergency. Our AI provider withdrew the model we were using, mid-project. Because the services depend on an interface rather than on a specific model, we swapped models with a configuration change and zero code modification. Without the layering that would have been a rewrite.”

What is the error envelope?

“Every error the API returns has the same shape: a code, a human message, details, and a request ID. The request ID lets us find that exact request in the logs. It means the frontend never has to guess what an error looks like, and we never leak internal details like stack traces to a user.”

How do you stop one user reading another user's contract?

“Ownership is checked on the server for every draft and every export — not hidden in the interface. If my contract is 41 and I ask for 42, I am refused. We have tests that attempt exactly that.”

Your audit log is tamper-proof. Prove it.

“I would run our tamper test. It deliberately goes into the database, corrupts a record in the middle of the chain, and then calls our verify endpoint — which reports the break and its location. We demonstrate the claim rather than assert it. And to be precise: the chain does not prevent tampering, it makes tampering detectable.”

Why did you keep the audit records when a user asks to be deleted?

“Because deleting links from a hash chain destroys the very property that makes it valuable — the ability to prove nothing was altered. The chain stores an actor and an action, never contract content or personal details. So we erase personal data and content, and retain an integrity ledger.”

What happens if your database goes down?

“Then the product is down, and I will not pretend otherwise — the database is a genuine single point of failure. In production it is a managed service with backups and automatic failover, which is why we chose a managed provider rather than running our own. A multi-region setup was explicitly out of scope, and it is listed in our future work.”

Why Clerk? Why not build login yourself?

“Because if we do not store passwords, we cannot leak them. Building authentication means getting password hashing, session expiry, reset flows and social login all correct, and each one is easy to get subtly wrong. We removed the whole category of risk by design rather than by being careful.”

What was the hardest bug you personally solved?

“On the serverless platform, application startup hooks did not fire the way they do on a normal server, and our HTTP client ended up bound to an event loop that had already closed. It produced intermittent failures that were very hard to reproduce. The fix was to build the client lazily per request and detect a new event loop, rather than assuming one-time startup. Diagnosing it meant reasoning about the platform's execution model, not just reading our own code.”

If you had three more months, what would you do first?

“Two things. First, retrospectively clean the rows orphaned by deletions performed before we fixed the purge — our fix governs deletions from now on and does not clean historical residue, and I would rather say that than pretend otherwise. Second, calibrate the confidence threshold statistically, so 0.7 can be defended with evidence rather than judgement.”

Part W — Your work, step by step (what you did, in order)

The question this answers

“What did YOU actually do, and in what order?” — and its harder follow-up, “why did you do it in that order?”

Most students answer this badly. They describe the finished system instead of their own sequence of work, which makes it sound like the project appeared fully formed — or worse, like somebody else built it and they are reciting it.

Tell it as a story with causes. Each step must explain why it had to come then, what went wrong, and what it unlocked. That is what a real engineer's memory of their own work sounds like.

Your one-line ordering rule (say this if asked why this order)

“I worked backend-first and bottom-up: nothing above a layer could be built until the layer beneath it was stable. The API contract was frozen first precisely so that Awais could build screens against something that would not move under him.”

My ten steps, in the order I actually did them

Step 1 — Froze the API contract before writing a single feature

What I did: I defined the exact shape of every request and response — what a Finding looks like, what an error looks like — and locked it. It is recorded in the code as a frozen contract version.

Why it had to come now: Because Awais had to build screens against my API. If I let its shape keep changing, he would rebuild his work every week. Freezing it early cost me some flexibility and bought the team a frontend that never had to be rewritten.

What went wrong: Twice I later wanted to change a response shape. I could not, so I had to add fields additively instead. That was mildly annoying and absolutely the right constraint.

What it unlocked: Awais could start designing against a stable contract, and Wasif could write tests against a specification rather than against my current mood.

Step 2 — Built the error envelope and request IDs

What I did: Every error the API returns has one shape: a code, a message, details, and a request ID that also appears in every log line for that request.

Why it had to come now: This is unglamorous plumbing and it had to come before the features, not after. Retrofitting consistent error handling into forty endpoints is far harder than starting with it.

What went wrong: Nothing dramatic — but it is the reason we can debug a user's problem later. Without the request ID, investigating a report of ‘it broke’ is guesswork.

What it unlocked: Every later feature got error handling for free, and Wasif could assert on error codes instead of matching error text.

Step 3 — Designed the database schema and wrote the migrations

What I did: Fifteen tables: the User → ContractDraft → ClauseFinding spine, plus the review workflow, the audit chain, and the caches.

Why it had to come now: The data model constrains everything above it. Getting it wrong means rewriting the services later, so it came before the services, not alongside them.

What went wrong: I initially had no separate ClauseFinding table — I was going to store the findings as a blob of JSON inside the contract row. That would have made it impossible to query ‘show me every critical finding across all contracts’, which the review queue needs. I split it out.

What it unlocked: The review queue, the audit chain, and the admin statistics all became possible — each of them needs to query findings independently of their contract.

Step 4 — Built the upload and validation path

What I did: File-size caps, extension allowlist, magic-byte inspection (reading the file's real first bytes rather than trusting its name), filename-traversal guards, and a SHA-256 content hash.

Why it had to come now: It is the front door. Everything downstream assumes it is handling a real, safe document, and that assumption has to be made true at the boundary.

What went wrong: My first version trusted the file extension. Wasif immediately broke it by renaming a file to .pdf. That is exactly what he is for, and it is why the magic-byte check exists.

What it unlocked: Taha's analysis code could assume it was always given a genuine, readable document, so he never had to defend against garbage input.

Step 5 — Wired up persistence, and the analysis endpoint

What I did: The endpoint that ties it together: accept the upload, call Taha's analysis services, store the draft and its findings, and return them.

Why it had to come now: This is the first point at which the product does something. It needed steps 1 to 4 to exist first.

What went wrong: The response was slow — the founder stared at a spinner for twenty seconds. We later changed it to return the clause findings first and enrich with the sweeps asynchronously.

What it unlocked: There was now a real, working product to build screens on top of. The backend-readiness gate could open.

Step 6 — Built the review queue with an atomic claim

What I did: The queue of findings that the system refuses to auto-approve, and the mechanism by which a reviewer claims one.

Why it had to come now: It is the enforcement point for our central safety promise: risky findings must reach a human. Without it, the confidence gate is a suggestion rather than a rule.

What went wrong: My first attempt was check-then-update — look to see if it is unclaimed, then claim it. That is a race condition: two reviewers can both pass the check in the same instant. I replaced it with a conditional update, so the database itself guarantees only one wins.

What it unlocked: The human-in-the-loop guarantee became real, and Wasif could write a concurrency test that proves it.

Step 7 — Built the SHA-256 hash-chained audit log

What I did: Every consequential action writes a record containing a fingerprint of the record before it, so the records form a chain.

Why it had to come now: It had to come before the export and admin features, because those are exactly the actions that must be accountable.

What went wrong: The subtle part was the first record — a chain needs a starting point, a genesis record with no predecessor. Getting that boundary case right took a while.

What it unlocked: We can now detect tampering, and Wasif could write the test I am proudest of: corrupt a record in the middle and assert we catch it.

Step 8 — Deployed it, and fought the serverless event loop

What I did: Docker, a managed Postgres database, automatic deployment from the main branch.

Why it had to come now: A product that only runs on my laptop is not a product. Deploying early meant we found the deployment problems early, when there was still time.

What went wrong: The hardest bug of the project. On the serverless platform, application startup hooks did not fire the way they do on a normal server, and our HTTP client ended up attached to an event loop that had already closed. It failed intermittently, which is the worst kind. The fix was to build the client lazily per request and detect a fresh event loop instead of assuming one-time startup.

What it unlocked: A live URL that the team, and an examiner, can actually visit.

Step 9 — Hardened the security, after an audit of my own work

What I did: Ownership checks on every object (IDOR), an SSRF guard on the URL analyser, secret scanning in CI, and rotated every credential.

Why it had to come now: Deliberately late. You cannot meaningfully audit a system that does not exist yet — and auditing it while it was still changing shape would have meant auditing it twice.

What went wrong: The audit found a real IDOR hole: an ownership check I had put in the interface but not enforced on the server. That is precisely the mistake I now warn about in my viva answers, and I found it in my own code.

What it unlocked: The security claims in our report became things we had tested, rather than things we had assumed.

Step 10 — Fixed the deletion purge and built the Clerk webhook

What I did: Made account deletion actually erase everything, from both deletion paths, sharing one purge routine.

Why it had to come now: The very last thing, because it was triggered by a bug Awais and I found together — a deleted user coming back when the same email was reused.

What went wrong: Two problems, not one. Deletion was leaving rows behind in six tables. And deleting from Clerk's own dashboard purged nothing in our database, because no webhook existed. I also had to make the webhook fail closed: if the signing secret is missing, it refuses everything — otherwise the URL is a remote account-deletion button for anyone who finds it.

What it unlocked: ‘Deleted’ finally means deleted, and the story became one of the strongest things we can tell an examiner.

If asked: "which step was hardest?"

“The serverless event-loop bug, without question. It failed intermittently, which means you cannot trust any single test run to tell you whether you have fixed it. And the cause was not in my code at all — it was in my assumption about how the platform starts an application. Fixing it meant reasoning about somebody else's execution model rather than reading my own lines.”

If asked: "what would you do differently?"

“I would key private data on the account ID from day one, rather than discovering the problem through a bug. And I would write the deletion purge at the same time as each feature that stores data — because every table we added was another place we later had to remember to erase. Deletion should have been a checklist item on every new table, not a cleanup task at the end.”

Part S — Scenario questions (“now add this…”)

Why this section exists

At some point the examiner will stop asking what you built and start asking what you would do next. For example:

  • “Add a feature that lets two founders share a contract.”
  • “How would you test this differently?”
  • “What if a contract is 500 pages long?”
  • “What if I told you the AI provider is shutting down tomorrow?”

These questions are not traps — they are gifts. They are testing whether you understand your own system well enough to extend it. A student who only memorised what they built will freeze. You will not, because you have a method.

The four-move method (use it for EVERY scenario question)

The four moves, spelled out

Move 1 — Clarify. Repeat the request back in your own words, then ask exactly one sharp question. It buys you ten seconds to think, and it makes you sound like an engineer rather than a student guessing. Example: “So founders can share a contract with a co-founder. Can I check — should the second person be able to edit the findings, or only read them? That changes the design.”

Move 2 — Anchor it in what already exists. Never design from a blank page. Name the part of Clarifyd it attaches to. “This builds on our existing review queue, which already assigns a finding to a person and checks their role.” This proves you know your own system.

Move 3 — Design in a fixed order: data → endpoint → screen → test. Always that order. It shows a layered mind. What table or column changes? What API endpoint appears? What does the user see? How do I test it?

Move 4 — Safety and trade-off. Finish by saying what could go wrong and what you would sacrifice. “The risk here is that sharing weakens our ownership check, so I would enforce it on the server, not just hide the button. And I would accept slower page loads rather than cache another person's findings in the browser.”

If your mind goes blank — the universal opener

“Let me think about that in layers — what data would need to change, what endpoint would expose it, what the founder would see, and how I would test it. Starting with the data…”

Three fatal mistakes in scenario answers
  • Saying “that would be easy.” Nothing is easy, and the examiner will immediately find the hard part you missed. Say instead: “The straightforward part is X. The part I would be careful about is Y.”
  • Inventing a technology you have never used. If you say “I'd use Kafka” you will be asked what Kafka is. Design with the tools you actually know and can defend.
  • Forgetting the safety rules you already built. Any new feature must still pass the confidence gate, the audit log, the ownership check, and the “not legal advice” rule. Mention them. It shows the safety thinking is habitual, not decorative.

Worked scenarios — backend, data and security

Add a feature: a founder should be able to share one contract with their co-founder, read-only.

1. Clarify: “So a second person gets read access to one specific contract. Can I check — read-only, or should they also be able to accept findings? That changes whether I touch the review workflow at all.”

2. Anchor: “This builds on what we already have: every contract has an owner_id, and every endpoint already performs a server-side ownership check. Right now that check asks ‘are you the owner?’. Sharing means it must ask ‘are you the owner or have you been granted access?’”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Data: a new table contract_share — draft_id, grantee_user_id, permission (read/write), granted_by, granted_at. I would not put a list of user IDs in a column on the contract; a join table is correct here because the relationship is many-to-many and I want to record who granted it and when. Endpoint: POST /drafts/{id}/share and DELETE /drafts/{id}/share/{user}, both owner-only. Then I change the single authorisation helper that every route already calls, so sharing is enforced in one place rather than scattered across twenty endpoints. Screen: a Share button on the findings page. Test: user B cannot read the contract; owner shares it; user B can now read but a write attempt is still refused; owner revokes; user B is refused again.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “The danger is that sharing weakens our IDOR protection — the check that stops you reading contract 42 by guessing. So the permission check stays on the server, in the one shared helper, and never in the interface. I would also append an audit event for every grant and revoke, because ‘who gave whom access to this contract, and when’ is exactly the kind of question our audit chain exists to answer. The trade-off: one extra database lookup on every request. I would accept that; correctness beats a millisecond.”

If pushed further: “If they ask about revocation timing: our access tokens are short-lived, about a minute, and the permission is checked from the database on every request — so a revoke takes effect immediately, not when the token expires.”

What if a founder uploads a 500-page contract? Your system scores 8 clauses at a time.

1. Clarify: “So the concern is a document far larger than we designed for. Can I check whether you mean it would be slow, or that it would fail? Both are real, and they have different fixes.”

2. Anchor: “Today we cap uploads at 25 MB, and we score eight clauses concurrently, paced by a rate limiter. A 500-page contract might be 2,000 clauses. At eight at a time, with the provider's rate limit, that is not a crash — it is a very long wait, and the browser request would time out first.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Data: the draft already has a status field, so I would add queued and processing. Endpoint: change analysis from synchronous to a job — POST /analyze returns a job ID immediately, and GET /jobs/{id} reports progress. Screen: the founder sees a progress bar and can leave the page; our frontend already keeps analysis alive across navigation, so this fits. Test: submit a synthetic 2,000-clause document and assert every clause is scored, none silently dropped.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “The real danger is the failure we already had once: under rate-limiting, findings were being silently discarded. A long contract makes that far more likely. So the job must be resumable and must count clauses in and out, and a mismatch must be an error, not a shrug. I would also warn the founder that a 500-page master agreement genuinely needs a lawyer — and I would rather say that than take their money for a twenty-minute analysis they should not rely on.”

Your database goes down at 2am. What actually happens, and what would you change?

1. Clarify: “Honestly? The product goes down. I am not going to pretend we have a hot standby that we do not have.”

2. Anchor: “The database is a genuine single point of failure. We reduced the risk by using a managed Postgres service with automated backups and failover, rather than running our own on a box — but that is the provider's failover, not ours.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “If I had to harden it: read replicas so that viewing existing findings survives a primary failure, even if uploading new ones does not — degrade to read-only rather than to nothing. Then a health check that returns a clear ‘temporarily unavailable’ page instead of a stack trace. Test: kill the database in a staging environment and assert the app returns a clean 503, not a leak of internal errors.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “The honest trade-off is cost. Multi-region redundancy was explicitly out of scope for a student project, and it is listed in our limitations. What I would not accept is failing ugly — leaking stack traces or hanging forever. Failing cleanly and visibly is affordable; failing invisibly is not.”

Add role-based permissions: a founder, a lawyer, and an accountant should see different things.

1. Clarify: “So different views of the same contract by role. Can I check whether this is about hiding data from some roles, or about who may act — who can accept a finding?”

2. Anchor: “We already have roles: viewer, reviewer, admin, enforced by a require_role check on the endpoints. So the machinery exists. What is being asked is a richer role model, not a new concept.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Data: today the role lives on the user. For per-contract roles I would move it onto the sharing table from the earlier scenario — a person's role is relative to a contract, not global. Endpoint: the existing role check gains a contract-scoped variant. Screen: the accountant sees only payment and liability clauses. Test: assert the accountant's API response does not contain the other clauses at all.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “The trap here is hiding clauses in the interface while the API still returns them. That is not security — anyone can open the browser tools and read the response. The filtering must happen server-side, and the test must assert that the data is absent from the response, not merely invisible on the page.”

Someone steals a user's access token. What can they do, and for how long?

1. Clarify: “A serious question, and I will answer it precisely rather than reassuringly.”

2. Anchor: “Our tokens come from Clerk and are short-lived — roughly one minute. They are sent over HTTPS only. The backend verifies the signature on every request using Clerk's public key.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “With a stolen token an attacker can act as that user until it expires — read their contracts, export a report. They cannot escalate to admin, because the role is checked server-side on every request. They cannot forge a new token, because they do not have Clerk's private key. Every action they take is written into our audit chain with their user ID and a timestamp, so the damage is at least attributable.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “What I would add: token revocation on demand, so an admin can kill a session immediately rather than waiting a minute; and rate-limiting per user to blunt bulk exfiltration. The honest limit is that a stolen valid token, within its lifetime, is indistinguishable from the real user — which is exactly why the lifetime is one minute and not one month.”

The examiner opens your code and points at one function. "Explain this to me."

1. Clarify: Do not panic, and do not pretend.

2. Anchor: “Read the function name and the signature aloud, slowly. That alone usually tells you what it does — we named things carefully for exactly this reason.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Then explain it in the order: what goes in, what comes out, what it protects against. For example: ‘This takes the raw uploaded bytes and the claimed filename. It returns a validated file or raises an error. It exists because the filename is attacker-controlled, so it checks the actual leading bytes instead.’”

4. Safety & trade-off: “If it is genuinely a teammate's code: ‘That is Taha's module — what I can tell you is what it guarantees to my layer: it always returns a validated finding, or it raises. My code never sees unvalidated model output.’ Knowing the contract between your module and theirs is exactly what a backend engineer is supposed to know, and saying it that way is a strong answer, not an evasion.”

Last page — What to say when you are stuck

Never say “I don't know.” Use one of these instead. Read them twice. They are all honest.

1. It is a teammate's area:
“That is [name]'s area and he can give you the exact detail — what I can tell you is how it connects to my part…” then say what you do know.

2. You genuinely do not know a fact:
“I don't want to guess at a number and mislead you. I know where it is in the code / report and I can show you in a moment. The principle behind it is…”

3. They found a real weakness:
“That is a fair criticism and we listed it ourselves in our limitations. Here is why we accepted that trade-off…”
Never argue with a true criticism. Agreeing, then explaining the trade-off, is a strong answer. Defending the indefensible is a weak one.

4. You did not understand the question:
“Can I check I've understood — are you asking about X or about Y?”
Asking for clarification is confidence, not weakness. Answering the wrong question is far worse.

5. They ask for something we did not build:
“We didn't build that, and it is in our future-work section. We scoped deliberately — Simple, Loveable, Complete — to finish one workflow properly rather than half-build several.”

6. Your mind goes blank:
“May I take a moment to structure that answer?” — then breathe, and start with the problem it solves. The rest will come.

Three things never to say
  • “The AI does it.” — This sounds like you do not know how your own system works. Say what the AI is asked, and what happens to its answer afterwards.
  • “It's 100% accurate / it's secure.” — Absolute claims invite an examiner to find the counterexample, and they will. Say what you measured, and how.
  • “We ran out of time.” — Say instead: “We scoped it out deliberately, and here is why.” A decision beats an excuse every time.
