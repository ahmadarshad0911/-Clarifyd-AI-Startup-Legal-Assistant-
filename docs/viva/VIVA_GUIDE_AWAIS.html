Part 2 — Project basics (every member must know these)

What is Clarifyd? (in one breath)

In simple words

A contract is a legal agreement — for example, the paperwork an investor gives a young company when they invest money in it. These documents are long, written in difficult language, and one bad sentence buried on page nine can cost the business owner everything they own.

Our users are founders — people starting a small company. They usually cannot afford a lawyer, and lawyers are slow. So founders often sign these documents without truly understanding them.

Clarifyd is a website. The founder uploads their contract. Our system reads it, finds the dangerous parts, and explains each one in normal everyday English — like a knowledgeable friend, not a lawyer.

Say this

“Clarifyd is an AI contract risk analyzer for pre-seed startup founders. A founder uploads a SAFE, an NDA or a term sheet. We extract the clauses, score each one for risk and confidence, sweep the document for loopholes — including protections that are missing — and explain every finding in plain English. It is decision-support. It is never legal advice.”

The user's journey (memorise this chain)

The eight-step pipeline (know the order)

1. Validate the file (read its real bytes) → 2. Extract the text → 3. Is it even a contract? → 4. Split into clauses → 5. Score each clause with the AI (8 at a time) → 6. Three whole-document sweeps → 7. Rank the findings → 8. Save, route risky ones to a human, write an audit record.

Memory trick: Validate, Extract, Gate, Split, Score, Sweep, Rank, Record — “Very Easy Girls Sing Sweet Songs, Rank Records.”

The four safety controls (the heart of the project — ANY member may be asked)

ControlWhat it means in one sentence
Fallback chainMain AI → second AI → a plain rules engine that needs no internet. The product never fails completely.
Injection defenceThe contract was written by the other side and may contain hidden instructions aimed at our AI. We treat it as hostile.
Confidence gateIf the AI is less than 0.7 sure, or the clause is critical, or foul play is suspected — a human must check it.
Hash-chained audit logEvery action is recorded so that secretly editing the past becomes detectable.

Plus: every AI answer carries a “not legal advice” label that cannot be removed — strip it and the response fails validation and never reaches the user.

Part 3 — Topic 1: What the frontend actually is

In simple words

The frontend is the part of a website that runs inside the user's own browser — the screens, the buttons, the text. The backend is a program running on a server far away that does the heavy work.

They talk to each other over the internet. When a founder drops a contract onto our page, my code packages that file up and sends it to Ahmad's backend, then waits, then displays whatever comes back.

An important consequence: anything in the frontend is visible to the user. They can open the developer tools and read every line of my code. So the frontend can never be trusted to enforce security. Hiding a button does not protect anything — the real check must happen on the server.

Say this

“The frontend runs inside the user's browser, so the user can read every line of it. That means the frontend can never enforce security — it can only present it. Hiding a button stops nobody. Every real check — is this your contract, are you allowed to export this — happens on the server. I present the rules; Ahmad's backend enforces them.”

Why this matters

Examiners love this question because many students genuinely believe that hiding a button is a security measure. Saying clearly that the frontend cannot be trusted proves you understand the client-server boundary.

Part 4 — Topic 2: The design system

In simple words

A design system means defining every colour, spacing size and text size once, in one place, and then reusing those definitions everywhere — instead of picking a colour fresh on every screen.

Without one, a website drifts: this page's grey is slightly different from that page's grey, buttons are three different heights, and the product feels amateur without anyone being able to say quite why.

Ours is called Broadsheet — a brutalist editorial style, like a serious newspaper. Warm ivory paper, coffee-black ink, and a single red accent used sparingly. Sharp edges, no gradients, no glassy effects. The look is deliberate: a founder is about to sign something serious, and the interface should feel trustworthy and calm, not playful.

Say this

“Every colour and spacing value is defined once as a token and reused. Change it in one place and the whole product follows. That is why it looks consistent — and, in fact, our viva presentation uses the same design system as the product itself.”

Two engineering problems I solved on the screens

In simple words

Problem one: the analysis takes 20 seconds, and users navigate away.

A naive website would throw the work away the moment the user leaves the page and start again when they come back. I put the analysis state above the page, in a shared context that outlives any single screen. So the founder can wander off to another page, and the work carries on, with a progress indicator that follows them.

Problem two: the login library is heavy.

Our authentication library is around 220 KB of code — a real download cost. But our public pages — the landing page, pricing, FAQ — do not need anyone to be logged in. So those pages render under a login-free stub and never download it at all. The public site is measurably faster as a result.

Part 5 — Topic 3: The privacy bug (your headline story)

Trap — be careful here

Volunteer this. Do not wait to be asked. It is the strongest story any of us has, because it is true, because we found it ourselves, and because the fix required understanding why we were wrong, not just what to change.

In simple words

Browsers can store small pieces of data on your own computer, so that a website remembers things between visits. This is called local storage. We used it to remember a founder's recent analyses, so the dashboard loads instantly.

Each stored item needs a label saying whose it is. We labelled them with the user's email address.

The symptom: we deleted a test user, then signed up again with the same email. The ‘new’ account was not new. It still had the old user's contracts, results, and even the old chat conversation.

The part that impresses examiners most: our first hypothesis was wrong

Say this

“Our first instinct was that the server was leaking deleted data. That instinct was wrong. The truth was the opposite: nothing had ever left the browser. The old data was still sitting on that machine, and the new account's label happened to match it. If we had chased our instinct, we would have spent days fixing a defect that did not exist.”

Say this

“An email address is not an identity. It is a label a person currently holds, and it can be reassigned. An account ID is permanent and never reused. We were keying private data on the reassignable thing, so we moved to the permanent thing. And on logout, or when a different account signs in, we now wipe every Clarifyd item on that device.”

Trap — be careful here

State the blast radius exactly. Do not exaggerate, do not minimise.

It affected the same physical browser only. A stranger signing up with that email on their own laptop would have seen nothing, because the leftover data lived on that one machine. It was a shared-device leak, not a server-side disclosure of contracts across the internet.

Examiners respect precision far more than drama. Overstating a bug is as dishonest as hiding it.

If pushed: Did you check whether the same mistake appeared anywhere else?

“Yes, and that is the important part. We found the same conceptual error in the database: the one-time sign-up code table was also keyed by email address, so a recreated account could inherit the deleted user's pending codes. Same root cause, different layer. Finding a bug twice tells you it was a thinking mistake, not a typo — so we fixed the thinking, not just the line of code.”

Part 6 — Topic 4: Clarifyd AI, and the button that refuses to work

In simple words

Clarifyd AI is the chat assistant. It answers a founder's follow-up questions and can draft a document with them, clause by clause.

Originally, the Generate document button was clickable from the moment the chat opened. So a founder could ask for a contract before answering a single question — and receive a document full of blanks reading ‘[TO BE CONFIRMED]’. Impressive-looking, completely useless.

Here is the interesting engineering problem: the screen has no way of knowing when enough detail has been gathered. It depends entirely on which document you are writing. A confidentiality agreement needs completely different details from a job offer. My code cannot possibly know that.

So we let the AI decide. It keeps asking questions, and only when it genuinely has every essential term does it add a hidden marker to its reply. My screen watches for that marker, removes it so the founder never sees it, and only then unlocks the button.

If pushed: How exactly do you detect that hidden marker?

“We match it loosely rather than exactly — and that was a deliberate decision worth explaining. The chat runs on a small, fast model that occasionally garbles the exact punctuation of the marker. A strict, exact check would be more ‘correct’ in a textbook sense — and it would trap a founder behind a button that never unlocks, with no way to understand why. We chose the user's outcome over the elegant implementation.”

Why this matters

That answer shows engineering judgement, which is rarer and more valuable than technical knowledge. Knowing that the strictly-correct solution is sometimes the wrong one — and being able to say why — is what separates an engineer from a coder.

Part 7 — The technologies you must be able to explain

Reacthow the screen is built
What it is (plain words)
A library for building user interfaces out of reusable components — small self-contained pieces like a button, a card, or a risk badge, which you assemble like Lego bricks.
Where it came from
Created at Facebook by Jordan Walke and released in 2013. Its central idea: rather than telling the browser how to change (‘find that box, change its text, add a red border’), you describe what the screen should look like for the current data, and React works out the minimal set of changes needed. That is called declarative rendering.
Why we chose it
Our screens change constantly — findings arrive, progress advances, chat messages stream in word by word. Managing all that by hand is where bugs breed. React makes the interface a function of the data.
What we rejected, and why
Plain JavaScript with manual updates was rejected: it does not scale past a few screens and is exactly where inconsistency bugs come from.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every screen: dashboard, findings, Clarifyd AI, exports, admin.
Next.jsthe framework around React
What it is (plain words)
A framework built on top of React that adds the things every real website needs: routing between pages, rendering pages on the server for speed, and image and font optimisation.
Where it came from
Created by Vercel (formerly Zeit) in 2016. Its key contribution is server-side rendering for React: the server sends a finished page, so the user sees content immediately instead of a blank screen while JavaScript downloads.
Why we chose it
Our public pages must load fast for a founder who has never heard of us and will leave in three seconds. Server-rendering them wins that. It also gives us a clean way to proxy our API calls, avoiding a whole class of cross-origin browser errors.
What we rejected, and why
Plain React (Create React App) was rejected: everything would be rendered in the browser, so the first paint would be slower on a poor connection.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
The entire frontend application.
TypeScriptcatching mistakes before the user does
What it is (plain words)
JavaScript with types added. You declare that a finding has a severity which is one of four specific words, and a confidence which is a number. If you then misspell a field or pass the wrong kind of value, the error appears while you are writing the code, not when a founder clicks the button.
Where it came from
Created by Microsoft in 2012 (led by Anders Hejlsberg, who also designed C#). It compiles down to ordinary JavaScript, so browsers do not need to know anything about it.
Why we chose it
This is the point worth making: our backend's API contract was frozen early. TypeScript lets me check at build time that my screens still match that contract. If Ahmad's response shape and my code ever disagreed, the build fails — it never reaches a user. Type-checking is our frontend quality gate.
What we rejected, and why
Plain JavaScript was rejected: mistakes would only surface at runtime, in front of the person using it.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Every frontend file. tsc --noEmit runs on every change and a failure blocks the merge.
Clerk (from the frontend side)the login, and the account ID that fixed our bug
What it is (plain words)
A specialist service that handles sign-up, sign-in, Google login, sessions and password reset. We never store a password ourselves.
Where it came from
Founded in 2020. It exists because almost everyone who builds their own login gets some part of it subtly wrong, and the consequences are severe.
Why we chose it
Two reasons. First: if we do not hold passwords, we cannot leak them — an entire class of vulnerability removed by design. Second, and crucially for my part: Clerk gives every account a permanent identifier that is never reused. That identifier is what we now use to label browser storage — it is the fix for the privacy bug in Part 5.
What we rejected, and why
Building our own login was rejected: password hashing, session expiry, reset flows and social login are each easy to get wrong, and a student project getting it wrong means leaking real people's credentials.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
The login page; a fresh signed token attached to every request; the account ID used to namespace local storage.
Browser local storagewhere the bug lived
What it is (plain words)
A small store of data that a website may keep on your own computer, so it can remember things between visits. It is per-site and per-browser: another website cannot read ours.
Where it came from
Standardised as part of HTML5 around 2009, to replace the practice of stuffing everything into cookies (which get sent to the server on every single request, making them slow and leaky).
Why we chose it
The dashboard should feel instant. Re-fetching everything from the server on every page load is slow, so we cache the results locally.
What we rejected, and why
Storing raw contract text in the browser was rejected on principle — we never persist the actual contract text in local storage, only the results. That is a privacy decision, taken deliberately.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Recent analyses, the founder profile, the chat session — all now namespaced by the permanent account ID and wiped on logout.
Framer Motionthe animation
What it is (plain words)
A library for animating React components — things fade and slide in rather than snapping into place.
Where it came from
Built by the Framer design tool team. It expresses animation declaratively: you describe the start and end states, not the frames between.
Why we chose it
Motion is used sparingly and for one purpose: to show a founder that something is happening during a twenty-second analysis. Everything respects the operating system's reduce motion setting, which matters for users with vestibular disorders — that is an accessibility requirement, not a nicety.
What we rejected, and why
Animating with hand-written CSS was rejected as harder to coordinate and easy to get wrong for accessibility.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Page entrances, the progress indicator, the streaming chat replies.

Part 8 — Question bank (cover the answers and test yourself)

Walk me through the interface, as a founder would experience it.

“They sign in, land on the dashboard, and drag a contract onto it. A progress indicator appears — and it follows them if they navigate away, because the analysis lives above the page. When it finishes they see a health gauge and a ranked list of findings, worst first. Each finding explains in plain English what the clause does and why it matters to a startup. They can open Clarifyd AI to ask follow-ups, and export a report.”

You hide the admin button from normal users. Is that security?

“No, and it is important that I say so. Hiding a button hides a door; it does not lock it. Anyone can read my frontend code in their browser. The real check is on the server: it refuses the request regardless of what my interface shows. The frontend presents the rules; the backend enforces them.”

How do you keep one user's data away from another's in the browser?

“Every stored item is labelled with the user's permanent account ID — never their email, because emails can be reassigned. And on logout, or when a different account signs in, we wipe every Clarifyd item on that device. We learned that the hard way, and I would like to tell you about it.” (then tell the bug story)

Do you store the contract text in the browser?

“Never. Only the results. The raw contract text is deliberately stripped before anything is cached locally. It is a privacy decision we took on purpose, not an accident of implementation.”

Why is the analysis able to survive navigation?

“Because the analysis state lives above any single page, in a shared context. A naive implementation ties the work to the page, so leaving it cancels the work. Ours keeps running and the progress indicator follows the user.”

Why does the Generate button start disabled?

“Because otherwise a founder gets a contract full of blanks. And the interesting part is that my screen cannot know when enough detail has been gathered — it depends on which document is being written. So the AI signals readiness with a hidden marker, and the interface unlocks only then.”

What is your frontend's testing story? Be honest.

“It is our weakest area and I will not dress it up. We have no frontend unit tests. Our only automatic gate is TypeScript type-checking, which does catch a real class of bug — if my screens stop matching the backend's frozen API contract, the build fails. But it is not a substitute for tests, and adding them is the fourth item in our future work.”

Are all your components actually used?

“No — and I would rather say it than have you find it. Several polished components exist that are not wired to a live route; the live pages reimplement equivalents inline. That is the gap between our component count and our shipped features, and it is listed in our limitations.”

Why does your design look like a newspaper?

“It is a deliberate choice. A founder is about to sign something that could cost them their company. The interface should feel serious, calm and trustworthy — like a broadsheet newspaper — not playful. Every colour, spacing and type size is a token defined once and reused, which is why it stays consistent across twenty screens.”

Last page — What to say when you are stuck

Never say “I don't know.” Use one of these instead. Read them twice. They are all honest.

1. It is a teammate's area:
“That is [name]'s area and he can give you the exact detail — what I can tell you is how it connects to my part…” then say what you do know.

2. You genuinely do not know a fact:
“I don't want to guess at a number and mislead you. I know where it is in the code / report and I can show you in a moment. The principle behind it is…”

3. They found a real weakness:
“That is a fair criticism and we listed it ourselves in our limitations. Here is why we accepted that trade-off…”
Never argue with a true criticism. Agreeing, then explaining the trade-off, is a strong answer. Defending the indefensible is a weak one.

4. You did not understand the question:
“Can I check I've understood — are you asking about X or about Y?”
Asking for clarification is confidence, not weakness. Answering the wrong question is far worse.

5. They ask for something we did not build:
“We didn't build that, and it is in our future-work section. We scoped deliberately — Simple, Loveable, Complete — to finish one workflow properly rather than half-build several.”

6. Your mind goes blank:
“May I take a moment to structure that answer?” — then breathe, and start with the problem it solves. The rest will come.

Three things never to say
  • “The AI does it.” — This sounds like you do not know how your own system works. Say what the AI is asked, and what happens to its answer afterwards.
  • “It's 100% accurate / it's secure.” — Absolute claims invite an examiner to find the counterexample, and they will. Say what you measured, and how.
  • “We ran out of time.” — Say instead: “We scoped it out deliberately, and here is why.” A decision beats an excuse every time.
