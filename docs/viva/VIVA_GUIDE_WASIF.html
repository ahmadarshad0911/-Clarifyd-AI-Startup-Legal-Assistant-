Part 2 — Project basics (every member must know these)

What is Clarifyd? (in one breath)

In simple words

A contract is a legal agreement — for example, the paperwork an investor gives a young company when they invest money in it. These documents are long, written in difficult language, and one bad sentence buried on page nine can cost the business owner everything they own.

Our users are founders — people starting a small company. They usually cannot afford a lawyer, and lawyers are slow. So founders often sign these documents without truly understanding them.

Clarifyd is a website. The founder uploads their contract. Our system reads it, finds the dangerous parts, and explains each one in normal everyday English — like a knowledgeable friend, not a lawyer.

Say this

“Clarifyd is an AI contract risk analyzer for pre-seed startup founders. A founder uploads a SAFE, an NDA or a term sheet. We extract the clauses, score each one for risk and confidence, sweep the document for loopholes — including protections that are missing — and explain every finding in plain English. It is decision-support. It is never legal advice.”

The user's journey (memorise this chain)

The eight-step pipeline (know the order)

1. Validate the file (read its real bytes) → 2. Extract the text → 3. Is it even a contract? → 4. Split into clauses → 5. Score each clause with the AI (8 at a time) → 6. Three whole-document sweeps → 7. Rank the findings → 8. Save, route risky ones to a human, write an audit record.

Memory trick: Validate, Extract, Gate, Split, Score, Sweep, Rank, Record — “Very Easy Girls Sing Sweet Songs, Rank Records.”

Part 3 — Topic 1: What a test actually is, and why 124 of them

In simple words

A test is a small program whose only job is to check that another piece of our program behaves correctly. It runs by itself, in seconds, with no human watching.

The value is not that it proves the code works today — you could check that by hand. The value is that it keeps proving it tomorrow. When someone changes one part of the system and accidentally breaks a different part, the tests catch it within seconds, before it reaches a real user. That accidental breakage is called a regression, and preventing regressions is most of what a test suite is really for.

We have 124 tests across 19 files. They run automatically on every change.

Where the tests are concentrated tells you what we were most afraid of

Say this

“The distribution of our tests is a design statement in itself. The heaviest concentrations are on upload security, on the AI inventing facts, and on what happens when the model fails — because those are the three places where a defect would be both invisible and catastrophic. A crash is loud and easy to find. A quietly wrong risk score is not.”

Part 4 — Topic 2: The hardest problem — testing a non-deterministic AI

Trap — be careful here

This is the question you will be asked, and most students have no real answer:

“How can you test an AI when it gives a different answer every single time?”

You have an answer. It is the best answer in your entire guide. Learn it word for word.

In simple words

The problem is real. A normal test says: given this input, the answer must be exactly this. But an AI phrases things differently every time you ask. You cannot compare its words to a fixed expected string.

Our solution is to separate two different questions that everybody else confuses:

  • Behaviour — does the system do the right thing? This is completely predictable, and we test it strictly and automatically.
  • Quality — is the AI's judgement any good? This is statistical, and we measure it separately.
Say this

“We separate the two things. The behaviour is deterministic and we test it strictly — the response must have the right structure, the findings must come back in the right order, the disclaimer must be present, and if the AI fails the backup must engage. Those are yes-or-no facts, and we check them automatically using a fake model that we control, so the tests are fast and repeatable. The AI's quality is a completely different question, so we measure it separately against a set of contracts where we decided the correct answers ourselves, by hand. In one line: we do not test the model's prose — we test the envelope around it.”

If pushed: Why use a fake model in the tests? Isn't that cheating?

“No — it is the only way to test the failure paths at all. I cannot make the real AI go down on demand. So I substitute a stub that I can order to fail, or to return malformed nonsense, and then I assert that our system retries, then falls back to the second model, then falls back to the rules engine. Those paths are the most important in the system and they are unreachable without a controllable fake. It also makes the tests fast, free and repeatable — a test suite that costs money and gives different results each run is not a gate, it is a lottery.”

Why this matters

This answer alone can lift your testing mark. It demonstrates that you understand why mocking exists — not as a shortcut, but as the only way to exercise failure paths deliberately.

Part 5 — Topic 3: The test we are proudest of (the tamper test)

In simple words

Our system keeps a tamper-proof history log. Each record contains a fingerprint of the record before it, so the records form a chain. If somebody edits an old record, its fingerprint changes, the next record no longer matches it, and the chain visibly breaks.

That is the claim. Anybody can make a claim. My job was to prove it.

So I wrote a test that deliberately attacks our own system: it reaches into the database, corrupts a record in the middle of the chain, and then asks our verification endpoint whether the chain is intact. The test passes only if the system reports the break.

Say this

“We deliberately corrupt a record in the middle of the audit chain and then assert that our system reports the break. The security claim is demonstrated, not asserted. That is the difference between saying ‘it is secure’ and proving it — and I would rather show you the test than ask you to take my word.”

The quality gate

In simple words

A quality gate is an automatic checkpoint that a change must pass before it is allowed into the main codebase. Ours runs on every single change and has three parts:

  • the full test suite must pass;
  • the frontend type check must pass — if the screens no longer match the backend's frozen API contract, the build fails;
  • the secret scanner must find no passwords or API keys accidentally committed into the code.

If any of the three fails, the change is blocked. It cannot be merged, so it cannot reach a user. The gate is not advisory — it is a wall.

Say this

“The gate is not a suggestion, it is a wall. Tests, type-check and secret scan all run on every change, and a red gate blocks the merge. That is how ‘nothing broken reaches the founder’ stops being a slogan and becomes a mechanism.”

Part 7 — The technologies you must be able to explain

pytestthe testing framework
What it is (plain words)
The tool that finds our tests, runs them, and reports which passed and which failed.
Where it came from
Created by Holger Krekel, first released in 2004. It became the Python standard because it is far less ceremonious than the older style — a test is just a normal function with a plain assert statement, so tests read like ordinary code rather than like a framework.
Why we chose it
It handles asynchronous tests, which we need — our backend does many things concurrently, and a test framework that could not await them would be useless to us. It also has fixtures: reusable setup code, so every test can start from a clean database without repeating itself.
What we rejected, and why
Python's older built-in unittest was rejected: far more boilerplate for the same result.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
All 124 tests. Run with a single command, and automatically on every change.
Mocking / stubbinghow you test something you cannot control
What it is (plain words)
Replacing a real component with a fake one that you command. Ours pretends to be the AI provider and can be ordered to fail, to be slow, or to return nonsense.
Where it came from
The idea was formalised in the early 2000s in the test-driven-development community, which drew the crucial distinction between testing your own logic and testing somebody else's service.
Why we chose it
You cannot make the real AI go offline on demand. But the failure paths — retry, fall back to the second model, fall back to the rules engine — are the most important code in the whole system, and they are unreachable in a test without a fake you control. Mocking is what makes those paths testable at all.
What we rejected, and why
Calling the real AI in the test suite was rejected: it costs money, it is slow, it needs the internet, and it gives a different answer every run — that is not a gate, it is a lottery.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
The provider stub in the fallback tests; the injection tests; the retry-exhaustion tests.
Continuous Integration (CI)the wall
What it is (plain words)
A service that automatically runs all our checks every time anybody proposes a change. If any check fails, the change is blocked.
Where it came from
The practice was popularised by Martin Fowler and the extreme-programming movement around 2000. The core insight: integrating small changes constantly and verifying each one is far safer than integrating a large batch of changes rarely.
Why we chose it
Without CI, tests only run when somebody remembers to run them — and under deadline pressure, nobody remembers. CI removes human discipline from the equation entirely.
What we rejected, and why
Trusting each other to run the tests before pushing was rejected. It always fails, on every team, eventually.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Runs pytest, the frontend type-check, and the secret scan on every change.
Black-box vs white-box testingtwo ways of looking at the system
What it is (plain words)
Black box: you test through the public interface, without looking at the code inside. Give it an input, check the output against the requirement. White box: you look inside and deliberately exercise particular internal paths.
Where it came from
The terms come from classical software-engineering literature (Myers, The Art of Software Testing, 1979) and remain the standard vocabulary.
Why we chose it
We use black box for our 26 formal test cases, because each one maps directly to a requirement and its fit criterion — and, deliberately, they can be written by somebody who did not write the code. We use white box for the fallback chain, because ‘the second provider was invoked’ is not visible from outside; you have to look in.
What we rejected, and why
Using only one was rejected: black box alone cannot reach the fallback paths; white box alone drifts away from what was actually required.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Table 7.2 of our report is the black-box suite; the provider-chain tests are white box.
Gitleaks (secret scanning)keys must never enter the code
What it is (plain words)
A scanner that reads every proposed change looking for things that look like passwords, API keys or tokens, and blocks the change if it finds one.
Where it came from
Secret leakage is one of the most common and most damaging real-world security failures — keys committed to public repositories are harvested by automated bots within minutes.
Why we chose it
Because a leaked key cannot be un-leaked. Deleting it later does not help: it stays in the project's history forever, and it has already been copied.
What we rejected, and why
Relying on code review to spot secrets was rejected. Humans miss them; scanners do not get tired.
Where it appears in Clarifyd
Runs in CI on every change. After one audit we rotated every credential as a precaution.

Part 8 — Question bank (cover the answers and test yourself)

How can you test an AI that gives a different answer every time?

“We separate behaviour from quality. Behaviour is deterministic and tested strictly with a fake model — right structure, right ordering, disclaimer present, and the fallback must engage when the model fails. Quality is statistical, so we measure it separately against contracts we labelled by hand. We do not test the model's prose — we test the envelope around it.”

What does 124 tests actually prove?

“Not that the system is perfect — no test suite proves that, and I would not claim it. What it proves is that the behaviours we specified still hold after every change. Its real value is catching regressions: someone changing one thing and unknowingly breaking another. That is caught in seconds instead of in front of a founder.”

Which single test are you proudest of, and why?

“The tamper test. It deliberately corrupts a record in the middle of our audit chain and asserts that we detect it. Most tests confirm the system works; that one tries to break it. A test that attacks your own system is worth more than ten that flatter it.”

“It is the opposite — it is the only way to test the failure paths at all. I cannot make the real AI go down on demand. With a controllable stub I can force it to fail, then assert that we retry, then fall back to a second model, then fall back to the rules engine. What is your biggest testing gap? Be honest.

“The frontend has no unit tests. Its only automatic gate is TypeScript type-checking, which does catch a real class of bug — if the screens stop matching the backend's frozen API contract, the build fails — but it is not a substitute for tests. I own that gap. It is stated in our limitations and it is item four in our future work.”

How do you know the whole thing works end to end, not just the pieces?

“Two ways. The analyze-endpoint tests exercise the full path — upload, validate, extract, score, persist, route to review — through the real API rather than against a single function. And we have smoke checks against the deployed system. But I will be precise: we do not have automated browser-level end-to-end tests. That is the gap I just named.”

What is a regression, and why do you care so much about them?

“A regression is when a change to one part of the system unknowingly breaks a part that used to work. They are the most common way software rots. In a four-person team, where I may not understand every line somebody else wrote, the test suite is what lets us change code confidently instead of fearfully.”

Why does your CI block a merge instead of just warning?

“Because a warning is a suggestion, and under deadline pressure suggestions get ignored. A block is a mechanism. The point of a gate is that it does not depend on anyone being disciplined at 2am the night before a deadline.”

How did you test the account-deletion fix?

“With a regression test that reproduces the original bug: delete an account, re-register with the same email, and assert the new account inherits nothing. And a test that asserts no row in any user-owned table still references a deleted account. A bug without a regression test is a bug that will come back.”

Part W — Your work, step by step (what you did, in order)

The question this answers

“What did YOU actually do, and in what order?” — and its harder follow-up, “why did you do it in that order?”

Most students answer this badly. They describe the finished system instead of their own sequence of work, which makes it sound like the project appeared fully formed — or worse, like somebody else built it and they are reciting it.

Tell it as a story with causes. Each step must explain why it had to come then, what went wrong, and what it unlocked. That is what a real engineer's memory of their own work sounds like.

Your one-line ordering rule (say this if asked why this order)

“I tested from the outside in, and in order of DANGER: first the inputs an attacker controls, then the failures nobody would notice, then the ordinary features. I deliberately wrote the security tests before the happy-path tests, because a broken happy path is loud and a security hole is silent.”

My seven steps, in the order I actually did them

Step 1 — Set up the quality gate before writing a single test

What I did: Wired continuous integration so that on every change, the test suite runs, the frontend type-check runs, and a secret scanner looks for committed passwords or API keys. A failure blocks the merge.

Why it had to come now: Because a test that only runs when somebody remembers to run it does not exist. Under deadline pressure, nobody remembers. The gate had to be a mechanism, not a habit.

What went wrong: Getting it to run reliably took longer than expected — a test suite that fails randomly is worse than no suite at all, because the team learns to ignore it.

What it unlocked: From that day on, no broken change could reach the main branch. Everything I wrote afterwards had teeth.

Step 2 — Attacked the upload endpoint first (14 tests)

What I did: Disguised files, oversized payloads, filenames with traversal sequences, and empty or corrupt documents.

Why it had to come now: The upload is the only place an attacker can hand us arbitrary bytes. It is the front door, so it is where I started — before any feature testing.

What went wrong: I broke it immediately. Ahmad's first version trusted the file extension, so I renamed a non-PDF to .pdf and it sailed through. That is exactly what the magic-byte check now prevents.

What it unlocked: The front door became genuinely defended, and Ahmad had a concrete demonstration of why filenames cannot be trusted.

Step 3 — Tested the failure paths — using a fake AI

What I did: Replaced the real AI with a stub I control, then ordered it to fail, to time out, and to return malformed nonsense — asserting each time that we retry, then fall back to the second model, then fall back to the rules engine.

Why it had to come now: These are the most important paths in the system and they are unreachable in a test without a fake. I cannot make the real AI go down on demand, and I would not want a test suite that costs money and needs the internet.

What went wrong: The subtle case was a schema-invalid reply — the AI answering successfully but in the wrong shape. I asserted that we treat that as a failure, not as an answer. A confidently malformed reply is more dangerous than an error.

What it unlocked: We could honestly claim graceful degradation, because it is tested, not hoped for.

Step 4 — Wrote the tamper test — the one I am proudest of

What I did: A test that deliberately reaches into the database, corrupts a record in the middle of the audit chain, and then asserts that our verification endpoint reports the break.

Why it had to come now: Because the audit chain is our central security claim, and a claim without a demonstration is just a sentence in a report.

What went wrong: It felt strange to write a test whose job is to attack our own system. That is precisely why it is worth more than ten tests that flatter it.

What it unlocked: The security claim in our thesis is demonstrated, not asserted — and I can run it in front of an examiner.

Step 5 — Tested authorisation: IDOR and SSRF

What I did: User A attempting to read user B's contract by guessing its ID. And the URL analyser being pointed at an internal address to make our own server attack itself.

Why it had to come now: These are the two authorisation failures that a functional test would never catch, because from the attacker's point of view nothing looks broken — it just quietly works when it should not.

What went wrong: I found a real IDOR hole: the ownership check existed in the interface but was not enforced on the server. Hiding a button hides a door; it does not lock it.

What it unlocked: Both are now enforced server-side, with tests that attempt the attack and assert refusal.

Step 6 — Built the live benchmark — separately from the test suite

What I did: A harness that runs the real model against contracts where we decided the correct severity by hand, and reports how often it agrees.

Why it had to come now: Because the AI's quality cannot be tested the way behaviour is tested. It is statistical, slow, costs money and gives different answers each run — so it must live outside the merge gate. A gate that is slow and flaky is a gate people learn to bypass.

What went wrong: Deciding the ground truth was genuinely hard: we had to agree what severity a clause deserved, and we did not always agree with each other. That disagreement is itself a finding worth admitting.

What it unlocked: We got a defensible number — roughly 89% exact-band agreement, always within one band — and, more importantly, Taha got a way to tell whether a rubric change made things better or worse.

Step 7 — Wrote the regression test for the privacy bug

What I did: Delete an account, re-register with the same email, and assert the new account inherits nothing. Plus: after deletion, no row in any user-owned table still references that account.

Why it had to come now: The moment a real bug is found, a test must be written that reproduces it — before the fix, not after.

What went wrong: The failing test was the proof we understood the bug at all. Until I could make it fail on demand, we were guessing at the cause — and our first guess had already been wrong.

What it unlocked: That class of bug can never silently return. A bug without a regression test is a bug that comes back.

If asked: "which step was hardest?"

“Deciding what ‘correct’ even means for the AI benchmark. For every other test there is a right answer: the file is rejected, or it is not. For the benchmark, we had to be the ground truth — and the three of us did not always agree on whether a clause was critical or merely high. Admitting that disagreement, rather than pretending our labels are objective, is the honest position.”

If asked: "what would you do differently?"

“Two things. I would add browser-level end-to-end tests — all 124 of my tests could pass while the product is completely broken, because a single wrong API URL in the frontend would not fail any of them. And I would run mutation testing: deliberately introduce bugs and check whether my tests actually catch them. It is the only honest way to test the tests.”

Part S — Scenario questions (“now add this…”)

Why this section exists

At some point the examiner will stop asking what you built and start asking what you would do next. For example:

  • “Add a feature that lets two founders share a contract.”
  • “How would you test this differently?”
  • “What if a contract is 500 pages long?”
  • “What if I told you the AI provider is shutting down tomorrow?”

These questions are not traps — they are gifts. They are testing whether you understand your own system well enough to extend it. A student who only memorised what they built will freeze. You will not, because you have a method.

The four-move method (use it for EVERY scenario question)

The four moves, spelled out

Move 1 — Clarify. Repeat the request back in your own words, then ask exactly one sharp question. It buys you ten seconds to think, and it makes you sound like an engineer rather than a student guessing. Example: “So founders can share a contract with a co-founder. Can I check — should the second person be able to edit the findings, or only read them? That changes the design.”

Move 2 — Anchor it in what already exists. Never design from a blank page. Name the part of Clarifyd it attaches to. “This builds on our existing review queue, which already assigns a finding to a person and checks their role.” This proves you know your own system.

Move 3 — Design in a fixed order: data → endpoint → screen → test. Always that order. It shows a layered mind. What table or column changes? What API endpoint appears? What does the user see? How do I test it?

Move 4 — Safety and trade-off. Finish by saying what could go wrong and what you would sacrifice. “The risk here is that sharing weakens our ownership check, so I would enforce it on the server, not just hide the button. And I would accept slower page loads rather than cache another person's findings in the browser.”

If your mind goes blank — the universal opener

“Let me think about that in layers — what data would need to change, what endpoint would expose it, what the founder would see, and how I would test it. Starting with the data…”

Three fatal mistakes in scenario answers
  • Saying “that would be easy.” Nothing is easy, and the examiner will immediately find the hard part you missed. Say instead: “The straightforward part is X. The part I would be careful about is Y.”
  • Inventing a technology you have never used. If you say “I'd use Kafka” you will be asked what Kafka is. Design with the tools you actually know and can defend.
  • Forgetting the safety rules you already built. Any new feature must still pass the confidence gate, the audit log, the ownership check, and the “not legal advice” rule. Mention them. It shows the safety thinking is habitual, not decorative.

Worked scenarios — testing and quality

Forget your test suite. How would you test this system a completely different way?

1. Clarify: “Happily — because our current suite has a real gap and this question walks straight into it.”

2. Anchor: “Today we test at the unit and API level: 124 automated tests plus a live-model benchmark. What we do not have is browser-level end-to-end testing, and I own that as our biggest gap.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Four other ways I would test it. One — end-to-end: drive a real browser through the whole journey (sign in, upload, wait, read findings, export) and assert the founder actually gets a report. That catches integration failures no unit test can. Two — property-based testing: instead of picking example inputs myself, generate thousands of random ones and assert an invariant always holds — for instance, the findings are always sorted by severity, no matter what document goes in. That finds edge cases a human would never think to write. Three — fuzzing the upload endpoint: throw malformed, truncated and hostile files at it and assert it never crashes and never leaks a stack trace. Four — load testing: fifty concurrent analyses and see what breaks first.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “If I could only add one, it would be end-to-end, because it tests the thing the founder actually experiences. My unit tests could all pass while the product is completely broken — a single wrong API URL in the frontend would do it, and not one of my 124 tests would notice.”

How would you test that the AI is not biased?

1. Clarify: “A sharp question, and I want to be careful because it is easy to give a glib answer here.”

2. Anchor: “First, what would bias even mean in our system? The most plausible form: the model rates the same clause as more or less risky depending on irrelevant surface details — the names of the parties, the jurisdiction named, the formatting.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “That is testable, and the method is a controlled experiment. Take one clause. Generate variants that differ only in an irrelevant attribute — the company name, a person's name, the country. Score all the variants. The severity should be identical. If it is not, we have measured bias, and we can quantify it. That is a proper experiment: one variable changed, everything else held constant.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “I have to be honest that we have not run this. It is a gap and I would rather name it than invent a result. I also would not claim that passing such a test means the model is unbiased — it means it is not biased along the axes I tested. Bias I did not think to look for would go unnoticed, and I will not overclaim.”

You have 124 tests. Prove they are actually worth anything. Couldn't they all be trivial?

1. Clarify: “A fair challenge — test count is a vanity number, and I would not defend it on its own.”

2. Anchor: “The right question is not how many, but what would slip through if they were deleted. So let me answer with the tests that would hurt most to lose.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “The tamper test deliberately corrupts our audit chain and asserts we detect it — without it, our central security claim is just a claim. The fallback tests force the AI to fail and assert we degrade to the rules engine — without them, we would not discover the product was dead until the provider went down. The injection test feeds in a malicious contract. The recreated-account test is a regression test for a real privacy bug we shipped and fixed. Every one of those tests attacks the system rather than confirming it.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “And the honest way to measure their worth: mutation testing. Deliberately introduce a bug — flip a comparison, delete a check — and see whether any test fails. If none does, the tests are decorative. We have not run a full mutation-testing pass, and I would put it high in our future work, because it is the only way to test the tests themselves.”

A user reports a bug you cannot reproduce. Walk me through what you do.

1. Clarify: “This is the everyday reality of QA, and our system was built to make it answerable.”

2. Anchor: “Every API response carries a request ID, and every log line carries that same ID. Every consequential action is in the audit chain with an actor and a timestamp.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Step one: get the request ID from the user — our error messages surface it, which is exactly why. Step two: pull every log line with that ID and reconstruct precisely what happened. Step three: check the audit chain for what that user actually did, rather than what they remember doing. Step four — and this is the important one: once I understand it, I write a failing test that reproduces it, and only then do I fix it. A bug without a regression test is a bug that comes back.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “If I genuinely cannot reproduce it, I do not close it as ‘works on my machine’. I add more logging around the suspect path and wait for it to happen again. The privacy bug we found was exactly this shape — it only appeared with a specific sequence of actions in one browser, and our first theory about the cause was wrong.”

Add a feature and tell me how you would test it: an email alert when a contract has a critical finding.

1. Clarify: “Can I check — immediately on analysis, or a daily digest? That changes what can go wrong, and therefore what I test.”

2. Anchor: “The trigger already exists. Our confidence gate already identifies critical findings and routes them to the review queue, so the decision point is built — I am adding an action to an event we already detect.”

3. Design (data → endpoint → screen → test): “Testing it has three layers. One — the trigger logic: given a critical finding, is an email requested? I test that with a fake email sender, asserting it was called with the right recipient and content. I do not send real email in tests. Two — the content: does the email contain the finding, and — crucially — the ‘not legal advice’ disclaimer? Our schema enforces that everywhere else, and an email must not become the one place it leaks out without it. Three — idempotency: if the analysis is retried, does the founder get two emails? That is the bug this feature will actually have, so I would test it specifically.”

4. Safety & trade-off: “The subtle danger is privacy. An email is not a private channel — it sits on servers we do not control. So I would put no contract content in it whatsoever — just ‘your contract has a critical finding, sign in to view it’. And it must respect deletion: if the account is deleted, any pending email must not go out. That is exactly the class of leftover our purge bug taught us to look for.”

If pushed further: “If they ask what could go wrong at scale: ‘Email sending is slow and can fail, so it must not block the analysis response. I would queue it, retry it, and make sure a failed email never fails the analysis.’”

Last page — What to say when you are stuck

Never say “I don't know.” Use one of these instead. Read them twice. They are all honest.

1. It is a teammate's area:
“That is [name]'s area and he can give you the exact detail — what I can tell you is how it connects to my part…” then say what you do know.

2. You genuinely do not know a fact:
“I don't want to guess at a number and mislead you. I know where it is in the code / report and I can show you in a moment. The principle behind it is…”

3. They found a real weakness:
“That is a fair criticism and we listed it ourselves in our limitations. Here is why we accepted that trade-off…”
Never argue with a true criticism. Agreeing, then explaining the trade-off, is a strong answer. Defending the indefensible is a weak one.

4. You did not understand the question:
“Can I check I've understood — are you asking about X or about Y?”
Asking for clarification is confidence, not weakness. Answering the wrong question is far worse.

5. They ask for something we did not build:
“We didn't build that, and it is in our future-work section. We scoped deliberately — Simple, Loveable, Complete — to finish one workflow properly rather than half-build several.”

6. Your mind goes blank:
“May I take a moment to structure that answer?” — then breathe, and start with the problem it solves. The rest will come.

Three things never to say
  • “The AI does it.” — This sounds like you do not know how your own system works. Say what the AI is asked, and what happens to its answer afterwards.
  • “It's 100% accurate / it's secure.” — Absolute claims invite an examiner to find the counterexample, and they will. Say what you measured, and how.
  • “We ran out of time.” — Say instead: “We scoped it out deliberately, and here is why.” A decision beats an excuse every time.
